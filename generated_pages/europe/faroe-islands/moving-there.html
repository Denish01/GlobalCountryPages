To move to the Faroe Islands, you will need to carefully plan and research the various requirements and options available, including visa applications, housing, and employment opportunities, as well as being prepared for the unique cultural and environmental aspects of this remote and rugged territory.

Why People Move to Faroe Islands

The Faroe Islands, with their stunning natural beauty and unique cultural heritage, attract a diverse range of expats, from outdoor enthusiasts and nature lovers to artists and writers seeking inspiration. The islands offer a high quality of life, with modern amenities, a strong sense of community, and a relatively low cost of living compared to other European countries. The expat community in the Faroe Islands is relatively small, but growing, with many people drawn to the islands' rugged landscape, rich cultural traditions, and friendly, welcoming locals.

The Faroe Islands have a distinct cultural identity, shaped by their Viking heritage and their historical ties to Denmark, and expats often comment on the strong sense of community and the high standard of living. The islands have a well-developed infrastructure, with good healthcare, education, and transportation systems, making it an attractive destination for families, professionals, and retirees alike. The unique blend of traditional and modern culture, combined with the breathtaking natural scenery, makes the Faroe Islands an exciting and inspiring place to live and work.

Despite its remote location, the Faroe Islands are well-connected to the rest of Europe, with regular flights to Denmark and other destinations, and a growing number of expats are taking advantage of the islands' strategic location to pursue careers in industries such as fishing, tourism, and renewable energy. The Faroe Islands are also a popular destination for digital nomads, with a growing number of co-working spaces and networking events, and a thriving startup scene.

Relocation Checklist

  1. Research the different types of visas and residency options available and determine which one is best for your situation.
  2. Gather all necessary documents, including a valid passport, birth certificate, and proof of language proficiency.
  3. Apply for a visa or residency permit through the Danish embassy or consulate in your home country.
  4. Find a place to live, either by renting an apartment or house, or by purchasing a property.
  5. Register with the local authorities and obtain a tax identification number.
  6. Open a bank account with a local bank and arrange for money transfers.
  7. Find a job or start a business, if applicable.
  8. Enroll in a language course to learn Faroese or Danish.
  9. Join online expat groups and social networks to connect with other expats and get advice and support.
  10. Plan for healthcare and insurance options, including private health insurance and access to the public healthcare system.
  11. Research the local culture and customs to avoid culture shock and to make a smooth transition to island life.
  12. Stay up-to-date with the latest news and developments in the Faroe Islands, including changes to visa requirements and residency rules.

Expat Communities

  • The capital city of Torshavn is the hub of expat activity, with many bars, restaurants, and cafes catering to international visitors and residents.
  • The online group Expats in the Faroe Islands is a popular forum for connecting with other expats and getting advice and support.
  • The Faroe Islands Expats Facebook group is another useful resource for meeting other expats and staying up-to-date with local news and events.
  • The International Club of the Faroe Islands is a social club that organizes events and activities for expats and locals alike.
  • The Faroe Islands Tourist Board website has a section dedicated to expats, with information on housing, jobs, and lifestyle.

Banking & Finances

  • Opening a bank account with a local bank, such as the BankNordik, is a straightforward process, but may require a tax identification number.
  • Money transfers can be arranged through online services, such as TransferWise, or through a local bank.
  • The Danish krone is the official currency, but many businesses also accept euros and credit cards.
  • Taxes are relatively high in the Faroe Islands, but the cost of living is also relatively low.
  • Health insurance is mandatory for all residents, and can be arranged through a private provider or the public healthcare system.

Challenges & Honest Advice

  • Language barriers can be a challenge, as Faroese is the primary language spoken, but many locals also speak Danish and English.
  • Culture shock can be a significant challenge, especially for those who are not used to the rugged and remote nature of the islands.
  • Bureaucracy can be slow and frustrating, especially when dealing with official paperwork and permits.
  • Weather can be harsh and unpredictable, with strong winds and frequent rain.
  • Job opportunities may be limited, especially in certain industries, and may require language proficiency and specialized skills.
  • Homesickness can be a challenge, especially for those who are far from family and friends, but the expat community is growing and supportive.
