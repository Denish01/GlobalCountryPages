# Doing Business in Albania — Investment, FDI & Opportunities

Doing business in Albania can be a relatively straightforward process, with the country offering a range of investment opportunities, particularly in the tourism, energy, and manufacturing sectors, although it also presents some challenges, including bureaucracy and corruption.

[SECTION] Business Environment [/SECTION]
Albania's business environment has undergone significant improvements in recent years, with the government actively working to attract foreign investment and promote economic growth. The country has implemented various reforms aimed at simplifying regulatory procedures, reducing bureaucracy, and increasing transparency. However, corruption remains a concern, and companies may still encounter difficulties in dealing with government institutions. The government's attitude toward foreign investment is generally positive, with a range of incentives and support available for investors. Despite these efforts, the business environment in Albania can still be challenging, with a relatively high level of bureaucracy and a need for patience and perseverance.

The government has established a number of institutions to support foreign investment, including the Albanian Investment Development Agency (AIDA), which provides a range of services, including investment promotion, business facilitation, and aftercare support. Additionally, the country has a relatively low corporate tax rate of 15%, which makes it an attractive location for businesses looking to establish a presence in the region.

Albania's strategic location, situated at the crossroads of Europe, Asia, and the Middle East, also makes it an important hub for trade and investment. The country has a long coastline along the Adriatic Sea, providing access to major shipping routes and facilitating the transportation of goods. Furthermore, Albania is a member of various international organizations, including the World Trade Organization (WTO) and the European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD), which provides access to a range of resources and support.

[SECTION] Foreign Direct Investment [/SECTION]
- Albania has attracted significant foreign direct investment (FDI) in recent years, particularly in the energy and manufacturing sectors.
- The main investing countries in Albania include Italy, Greece, and Turkey.
- The government offers a range of incentives to attract FDI, including tax breaks, subsidies, and investment grants.
- Restricted sectors for FDI include defense, telecommunications, and certain areas of the energy sector.
- The Albanian government has established a range of institutions to support FDI, including the Albanian Investment Development Agency (AIDA) and the Foreign Investment Promotion Agency (FIPA).

[SECTION] Legal & Regulatory Framework [/SECTION]
- Albania has a relatively well-developed legal and regulatory framework, with a range of laws and regulations in place to support business and investment.
- The country has a **Civil Code** and a **Commercial Code**, which provide a framework for business operations and investment.
- Intellectual property protection is provided through the **Law on Intellectual Property**, which includes provisions for the protection of trademarks, patents, and copyrights.
- Labor laws are governed by the **Labor Code**, which provides a range of protections for workers, including minimum wage and working conditions.
- Dispute resolution is provided through the **Civil Procedure Code**, which includes provisions for mediation, arbitration, and court proceedings.
- The Albanian government has also established a range of institutions to support the legal and regulatory framework, including the **Albanian National Chamber of Commerce** and the **Albanian Bar Association**.

[SECTION] Challenges & Risks [/SECTION]
- One of the main challenges facing businesses in Albania is bureaucracy, with a range of regulatory procedures and requirements that can be time-consuming and complex.
- Corruption is also a significant risk, with companies often encountering difficulties in dealing with government institutions and officials.
- The country's infrastructure, particularly in the transportation sector, is in need of development, which can make it difficult to transport goods and people.
- The Albanian economy is also vulnerable to external shocks, particularly in the energy sector, which can have a significant impact on the overall economy.
- Additionally, the country's **currency stability** is moderate, with the Albanian lek (ALL) subject to fluctuations in value.
- The business environment in Albania can also be affected by **political risk**, with the country's political situation sometimes unstable.

[SECTION] Free Zones & Incentives [/SECTION]
- Albania has established a range of **free zones** and **incentives** to attract investment, including the **Tirana Free Zone** and the **Vlorë Free Zone**.
- The government offers a range of tax incentives, including **tax breaks** and **subsidies**, to companies that invest in certain sectors or regions.
- The **Albanian Investment Development Agency (AIDA)** provides a range of support services to investors, including investment promotion, business facilitation, and aftercare support.
- The government has also established a range of **investment promotion agencies**, including the **Foreign Investment Promotion Agency (FIPA)**, to support investment and provide information to investors.
