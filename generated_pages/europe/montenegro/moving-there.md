# Moving to Montenegro — Visas, Residency & Expat Guide

To move to Montenegro, you should start by researching the country's visa and residency options, understanding the cost of living, and planning your relocation step by step, considering factors such as language, culture, and job opportunities.

[SECTION] Why People Move to Montenegro [/SECTION]
Montenegro is a beautiful country with a stunning coastline, a rich history, and a growing economy, making it an attractive destination for expats, digital nomads, and retirees. The country offers a high **quality of life**, with a pleasant Mediterranean climate, a vibrant cultural scene, and a relatively low cost of living. Many expats are drawn to Montenegro's stunning natural beauty, with its rugged mountains, pristine beaches, and crystal-clear waters. The country is also a popular destination for outdoor enthusiasts, with plenty of opportunities for hiking, skiing, and water sports.

The majority of expats in Montenegro are from neighboring countries, such as **Croatia**, **Bosnia and Herzegovina**, and **Serbia**, as well as from Western Europe and the United States. The expat community is relatively small, but growing, with many foreigners drawn to the country's relaxed lifestyle, beautiful scenery, and business opportunities. Montenegro is also a popular destination for **digital nomads**, with a growing number of coworking spaces and a relatively low cost of living.

The **cost of living** in Montenegro is relatively low compared to other European countries, making it an attractive destination for retirees and expats on a budget. The country has a relatively low crime rate, and the people are generally friendly and welcoming to foreigners. However, the country is still developing its infrastructure, and some areas can be quite **rural**, with limited access to amenities and services.

[SECTION] Expat Communities [/SECTION]
- The capital city, **Podgorica**, has a growing expat community, with many foreigners living and working in the city
- The coastal town of **Budva** is a popular destination for expats, with a vibrant nightlife and a growing community of foreigners
- The old town of **Kotor** is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and a popular destination for tourists and expats alike
- Online groups, such as Facebook groups and forums, provide a platform for expats to connect and share information
- Social networks, such as LinkedIn and Twitter, can be useful for connecting with other expats and professionals in the country

[SECTION] Banking & Finances [/SECTION]
- Opening a bank account in Montenegro is relatively straightforward, with many banks offering services in English
- Money transfers can be made through banks or online services, such as PayPal or TransferWise
- The local currency is the **Euro**, although the country is not a member of the Eurozone
- Credit cards are widely accepted, although cash is still the preferred method of payment in many places
- Taxes in Montenegro are relatively low, with a flat income tax rate of 9%

[SECTION] Challenges & Honest Advice [/SECTION]
- **Culture shock** can be a challenge for many expats, with Montenegro having a unique culture and way of life
- **Bureaucracy** can be slow and inefficient, with many administrative tasks requiring patience and persistence
- **Language barriers** can be a challenge, with many Montenegrins speaking limited English, especially outside of tourist areas
- **Job opportunities** can be limited, especially for foreigners, with many jobs requiring language proficiency and local qualifications
- **Infrastructure** can be limited in some areas, with poor roads and limited access to amenities and services
- **Integration** can be a challenge, with many expats finding it difficult to connect with the local community and make friends
