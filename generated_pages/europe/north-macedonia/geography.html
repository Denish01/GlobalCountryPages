Location & Borders

North Macedonia is a landlocked country located in the Balkan Peninsula in southeastern Europe. It is situated in the central part of the peninsula, bordered by five countries: Kosovo to the northwest, Serbia to the north, Bulgaria to the east, Greece to the south, and Albania to the west. The country's strategic location has made it an important crossroads between Europe and the Middle East. The Vardar River, which flows through the country, is the longest river in North Macedonia and plays a significant role in the country's geography and economy.

North Macedonia's borders are characterized by diverse landscapes, from the rugged mountains in the west to the fertile valleys in the east. The country's terrain has been shaped by the movement of tectonic plates, resulting in a complex geography with numerous valleys, mountains, and lakes. The border with Greece is the longest, stretching for over 240 kilometers, while the border with Albania is the shortest, at just over 150 kilometers. The country's location in the heart of the Balkans has made it an important center for trade and cultural exchange throughout history.

Terrain & Landscape

North Macedonia's terrain is characterized by a mix of mountains, valleys, and lakes. The country can be divided into three main regions: the mountainous region in the west, the central region with its fertile valleys, and the eastern region with its rolling hills. The mountainous region is home to some of the country's highest peaks, including Mount Korab, which is the highest point in North Macedonia. The central region is dominated by the Vardar River, which flows through the capital city of Skopje and provides a source of water and electricity for the country.

The country's landscape is also dotted with numerous lakes, including Lake Ohrid, which is one of the oldest and deepest lakes in Europe. The lake is a popular tourist destination and is known for its crystal-clear waters and stunning scenery. The country's forests are also an important feature of its landscape, covering over 30% of the country's land area. The forests are home to a wide range of flora and fauna, including bears, wolves, and eagles.

The country's terrain has played a significant role in shaping its history and culture. The mountains have provided a natural barrier against invaders, while the valleys have provided fertile land for agriculture. The country's lakes and rivers have also played an important role in the development of its economy, providing a source of water and electricity.
