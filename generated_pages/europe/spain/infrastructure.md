[SECTION] Infrastructure Overview [/SECTION]
Spain's infrastructure is considered one of the most developed in the world, with significant investments made in recent years to improve the country's transportation networks, energy systems, and telecommunications. The country's strategic location at the crossroads of Europe, Africa, and the Americas makes it an important hub for international trade and tourism. However, there is still a noticeable gap between urban and rural areas, with cities like Madrid and Barcelona enjoying better infrastructure and services than smaller towns and villages. Despite this, the Spanish government has been working to bridge this gap, with initiatives aimed at improving rural connectivity and access to basic services.

The country's infrastructure development has been driven by a combination of public and private investments, with a focus on **sustainability** and **innovation**. Spain has been at the forefront of renewable energy development, with a strong focus on **solar** and **wind power**. The country has also made significant progress in improving its transportation networks, with the development of high-speed rail lines and the expansion of its airport and port facilities.

Spain's infrastructure is also supported by a strong institutional framework, with a well-developed regulatory environment and a high level of transparency. This has helped to attract foreign investment and promote economic growth, making Spain an attractive destination for businesses and investors.

- Spain's energy sector is regulated by the **National Energy Commission**, which is responsible for promoting competition and ensuring a stable and secure energy supply.
