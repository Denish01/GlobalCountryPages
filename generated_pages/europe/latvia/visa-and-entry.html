Visa Policy Overview

Latvia has a moderate visa policy, allowing citizens of many countries to enter without a visa. As a member of the European Union and the Schengen Area, Latvia follows the common visa policy of the EU, which provides for visa-free travel for citizens of certain countries. Currently, citizens of over 60 countries can enter Latvia without a visa, including the United States, Canada, Australia, and the United Kingdom. These visa-free travelers can stay in Latvia for up to 90 days within a 180-day period.

The country's visa policy is designed to facilitate travel and tourism while maintaining national security and immigration controls. Latvia has a well-developed system for issuing visas, including electronic visas and visas on arrival, which makes it easy for travelers to plan their trips. The country's visa policy is regularly reviewed and updated to reflect changes in international relations, security concerns, and other factors.

By Visitor Type

Tourists: Tourists can enter Latvia without a visa if they are citizens of eligible countries. The typical stay is up to 90 days, and extensions are possible in exceptional cases. Tourists must have a valid passport, proof of sufficient funds, and health insurance that covers them for the duration of their stay.

Business Travelers: Business travelers may need a visa, depending on their nationality and the purpose of their visit. They may need to provide an invitation letter from a Latvian company, proof of business activities, and other documents. Business travelers can stay in Latvia for up to 90 days, and they may need to register with the local authorities.

Students: Students who want to study in Latvia must apply for a student visa. They need to provide proof of enrollment in a Latvian educational institution, proof of sufficient funds, and health insurance. Student visas are usually issued for the duration of the study program, and students may need to extend their visas periodically.

Workers: Workers who want to work in Latvia must apply for a work visa. They need to have a job offer from a Latvian employer, proof of qualifications and experience, and other documents. Work visas are usually issued for a specific period, and workers may need to extend their visas periodically.

Documents Checklist

  1. Valid passport with at least six months' validity
  2. Completed visa application form (if required)
  3. Recent passport-sized photos
  4. Proof of sufficient funds (bank statement, cash, etc.)
  5. Health insurance that covers the duration of stay
  6. Invitation letter from a Latvian company or organization (if required)
  7. Proof of enrollment in a Latvian educational institution (if applicable)
  8. Proof of qualifications and experience (if applying for a work visa)
  9. Police certificate (if required)
  10. Proof of accommodation in Latvia (hotel reservation, rental agreement, etc.)

Entry Points & Procedures

The main entry points to Latvia are Riga International Airport, which receives flights from many European cities, and the land borders with Estonia, Lithuania, Russia, and Belarus. When arriving in Latvia, travelers must go through immigration control, where they will need to present their passports and other required documents. The immigration officer will check the traveler's documents, ask questions about the purpose of their visit, and may request additional information. Travelers who need a visa must apply for it in advance, and they will need to present the visa at the border control.

Latvia has a well-organized system for handling arrivals, and the immigration process is usually straightforward. However, travelers should be prepared to wait in line, especially during peak travel seasons. It is also important to ensure that all required documents are in order to avoid any issues or delays.

Common Mistakes To Avoid

  • Not having a valid passport with at least six months' validity
  • Not having sufficient funds to cover the duration of stay
  • Not having health insurance that covers the duration of stay
  • Not registering with the local authorities (if required)
  • Not extending a visa or residence permit (if required)
  • Not having the required documents for a specific type of visa (e.g., student visa, work visa)
