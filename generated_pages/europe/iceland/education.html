Education System Overview

Iceland's education system is divided into four main levels: primary education (ages 6-10), lower secondary education (ages 11-14), upper secondary education (ages 15-19), and tertiary education. Primary and lower secondary education are compulsory for all children, and the curriculum includes subjects such as Icelandic, mathematics, science, and social studies. Upper secondary education is not compulsory, but most students choose to attend, and it prepares them for university studies or vocational training. The education system in Iceland is generally of high quality, with a strong emphasis on teacher training and student-centered learning. Most schools in Iceland are public schools, but there are also some private schools, mainly in the Reykjavik area.

The education system in Iceland is known for its flexibility and student autonomy, with students having a significant amount of freedom to choose their own courses and specialize in areas that interest them. This approach has been shown to be effective in motivating students and improving academic performance. Additionally, the education system in Iceland places a strong emphasis on equality and inclusion, with a focus on providing support services for students with special needs.

Overall, the education system in Iceland is well-regarded and has contributed to the country's high literacy rates and academic achievement. The system is also constantly evolving, with a focus on innovation and reform to ensure that it remains relevant and effective in preparing students for the challenges of the 21st century.

International Schools

  • International schools in Iceland offer a range of curricula, including the International Baccalaureate (IB), British, and American curricula.
  • These schools are mainly located in the Reykjavik area and cater to expatriate families and international students.
  • The cost of attending an international school in Iceland can be high, with tuition fees ranging from $5,000 to $15,000 per year.
  • Some international schools in Iceland also offer boarding facilities for students who live outside of the Reykjavik area.
  • Examples of international schools in Iceland include the International School of Reykjavik and the Reykjavik International School.

Studying as a Foreigner

  • To study in Iceland as a foreign student, you will need to obtain a student visa, which can be applied for at the Icelandic embassy or consulate in your home country.
  • Language requirements vary depending on the university and program, but most universities require proficiency in English or Icelandic.
  • There are scholarships available for international students, including the Icelandic Government Scholarship and the Erasmus Mundus Scholarship.
  • The cost of living in Iceland can be high, with accommodation, food, and transportation costs ranging from $800 to $1,200 per month.
  • Health insurance is also required for international students, and can be purchased through the Icelandic health insurance system or a private provider.
  • It is recommended that international students learn some Icelandic to facilitate their integration into Icelandic society and to improve their job prospects.

Education Quality & Challenges

  • The education system in Iceland is known for its high quality and emphasis on literacy and critical thinking.
  • However, the system also faces challenges, including funding constraints and inequality in access to education.
  • Recent reforms have aimed to improve the quality of education and to increase access to higher education.
  • The education system in Iceland is also adapting to the challenges of the 21st century, including climate change and technological advancements.
  • Overall, the education system in Iceland is well-regarded and effective in preparing students for the challenges of the future.
