# Cancun Culture - Food, Traditions & Etiquette

Cancun's culture is a unique blend of Mexican and Caribbean influences, shaped by its history, geography, and the diversity of its people.

[SECTION] Cultural Identity [/SECTION]
Cancun's cultural identity is defined by its rich Mayan heritage, its strategic location on the Caribbean coast of Mexico, and its modern status as a thriving tourist destination. The city's population is predominantly Mexican, with a significant proportion of indigenous Mayan people, as well as a growing expatriate community. The official language is Spanish, but many locals also speak Mayan dialects, such as Yucatec Maya, and some English, particularly in the tourism industry. Cancun's cultural identity is also influenced by its history as a major trading hub, with African, European, and Asian immigrants contributing to the city's cultural melting pot.

The city's cultural landscape is characterized by a blend of traditional and modern elements, with ancient Mayan ruins and colonial-era architecture standing alongside modern hotels, resorts, and shopping centers. The local population is known for its warm hospitality and welcoming nature, with a strong emphasis on family, community, and tradition. Cancun's cultural identity is also reflected in its vibrant arts scene, which includes traditional music, dance, and handicrafts, as well as contemporary art forms, such as painting, sculpture, and photography.

Cancun's cultural diversity is also evident in its many festivals and celebrations, which showcase the city's rich heritage and its connections to the wider world. From the colorful parades and processions of the Day of the Dead to the lively music and dance of the Cancun Jazz Festival, the city's cultural calendar is filled with exciting events and activities that reflect its unique cultural identity.

[SECTION] Etiquette: Do's and Don'ts [/SECTION]
**Do:**
- Respect local customs and traditions, particularly during festivals and celebrations
- Learn some basic Spanish phrases, such as "gracias" (thank you) and "por favor" (please)
- Tip servers and bartenders, as well as tour guides and other service providers
- Try local foods and drinks, such as tacos and horchata
- Support local businesses and artisans, such as handicraft vendors and small restaurants
- Be mindful of your surroundings and keep an eye on your belongings, particularly in crowded areas

**Don't:**
- Don't touch or remove artifacts from ancient Mayan ruins or other cultural sites
- Don't litter or dispose of trash improperly, as this can harm the local environment
- Don't take pictures of people without their permission, particularly in traditional or sacred contexts
- Don't drink tap water, as it may not be safe for consumption
- Don't ignore local warnings or advisories, such as those related to weather or safety
- Don't be disrespectful or rude to locals, as this can cause offense and harm relationships

- **Mayan handicrafts**: Traditional Mayan handicrafts, such as textiles, pottery, and woodcarvings, are highly prized for their beauty and cultural significance
