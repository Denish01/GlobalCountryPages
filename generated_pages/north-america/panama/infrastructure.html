Infrastructure Overview

Panama has made significant strides in developing its infrastructure, with a focus on improving transportation networks, energy generation, and telecommunications. The country has invested heavily in modernizing its road network, with a mix of highways, urban roads, and rural roads. However, there is still a notable urban-rural gap, with rural areas often lacking access to basic services such as electricity, water, and sanitation. Recent investments in infrastructure development have been driven by the government's efforts to promote economic growth, improve living standards, and enhance the country's competitiveness.

The Panamanian government has implemented various initiatives to address the infrastructure gap, including public-private partnerships and foreign investment. These efforts have resulted in the development of new transportation projects, energy generation facilities, and telecommunications infrastructure. Despite these advancements, challenges persist, particularly in rural areas where access to basic services remains limited.

Panama's infrastructure development has also been influenced by its strategic location, with the Panama Canal being a major shipping route and a significant contributor to the country's economy. The canal's expansion, completed in 2016, has increased the country's capacity to handle larger ships and has boosted trade and economic activity.

Energy & Electricity

  • Panama's power generation mix is dominated by hydroelectric power, which accounts for approximately 60% of the country's electricity generation.
  • The country has made significant progress in increasing renewable energy production, with a goal of reaching 70% of total energy production by 2050.
  • Energy efficiency measures have been implemented to reduce energy consumption and promote sustainable development.
  • Panama's power grid is relatively reliable, with an average of 2-3 power outages per year.
  • The government has launched initiatives to promote energy access in rural areas, where many communities lack access to electricity.

Water & Sanitation

  • Tap water is generally safe to drink in urban areas, but may not be potable in rural areas.
  • Sanitation coverage is relatively high, with over 90% of the population having access to improved sanitation facilities.
  • Water access is a significant challenge in rural areas, where many communities rely on rainwater harvesting or wells.
  • The government has implemented programs to improve water quality and increase access to wastewater treatment facilities.
  • Water conservation efforts are being promoted to reduce water waste and protect this vital resource.
