What Is US Virgin Islands?

The US Virgin Islands is an unincorporated territory of the United States, located in the Caribbean Sea. It is situated approximately 40 miles east of Puerto Rico, and is part of the Leeward Islands chain. The territory is known for its beautiful beaches, crystal-clear waters, and lush green mountains, making it a popular destination for tourists and travelers.

The US Virgin Islands is composed of three main islands: St. Thomas, St. John, and St. Croix. Each island has its own unique character and attractions, ranging from historic sites and museums to national parks and wildlife refuges. The territory has a rich cultural heritage, with a mix of African, European, and indigenous influences. The official language is English, and the US dollar is the official currency.

The US Virgin Islands has a strategic geographic position, with a tropical climate and a diverse economy. The territory is home to a number of industries, including tourism, finance, and manufacturing. The US Virgin Islands is also an important hub for international trade and commerce, with a number of major ports and shipping facilities. The territory's stunning natural beauty, rich culture, and strong economy make it an attractive destination for visitors and investors alike.

  2. 1718 - The Danish establish a settlement on St. John, and the island becomes a major producer of sugar and other crops.
  3. 1733 - The Danish purchase St. Croix from the French, and the island becomes a major commercial center.
  4. 1867 - The US offers to purchase the Danish West Indies, but the sale is rejected.
  5. 1917 - The US purchases the Danish West Indies, including St. Thomas, St. John, and St. Croix, for $25 million.
  6. 1927 - The US Virgin Islands is officially organized as an unincorporated territory of the United States.
  7. 1954 - The US Virgin Islands adopts a new constitution, which provides for a greater degree of self-government.
