Visa Policy Overview

Costa Rica has a relatively liberal visa policy, allowing citizens of many countries to enter without a visa. The country is a popular tourist destination, and its visa policy reflects this by providing easy access to visitors from around the world. Citizens of over 100 countries can enter Costa Rica without a visa, and many more can obtain a visa on arrival or through a simple online application process. This makes it an attractive destination for travelers, with many nationalities able to stay in the country for up to 90 days without a visa.

The visa policy in Costa Rica is designed to promote tourism and economic development, while also ensuring the safety and security of its citizens. The country has a long history of welcoming visitors, and its visa policy is just one aspect of its efforts to provide a warm and welcoming environment for travelers. With its stunning natural beauty, rich culture, and modern amenities, Costa Rica is an ideal destination for tourists, business travelers, and students alike.

Tourists: Tourists can enter Costa Rica without a visa and stay for up to 90 days. They must have a valid passport, proof of onward travel, and sufficient funds to support their stay. Tourists can extend their stay for an additional 90 days with proof of onward travel. Business Travelers: Business travelers may need to obtain a special visa, depending on the purpose and duration of their stay. They must provide an invitation letter from a Costa Rican company, proof of sufficient funds, and a valid passport. Students: Students must obtain a student visa, which requires proof of enrollment in a Costa Rican educational institution, a valid passport, and sufficient funds to support their stay. The duration of the student visa is typically one year, but can be extended with proof of continued enrollment. Workers: Workers must obtain a work visa, which requires sponsorship from a Costa Rican employer. The worker must provide a valid passport, proof of qualifications and experience, and a medical certificate.

The main entry points for Costa Rica are the Juan Santamaria International Airport (SJO) in San Jose and the Daniel Oduber Quiros International Airport (LIR) in Liberia. Land borders are also open, with entry points at Penas Blancas (Nicaragua) and Paso Canoas (Panama). Upon arrival, visitors must present their passport, proof of onward travel, and proof of sufficient funds to support their stay. Immigration officials may also ask questions about the purpose and duration of stay, as well as the visitor's accommodation and travel plans. Visitors must also complete a declaration form, which includes information about their luggage and any restricted items they may be carrying.
Costa Rica has a relatively straightforward entry procedure, and visitors can expect to be processed quickly and efficiently. However, it is essential to ensure that all required documents are in order and that the visitor has a clear understanding of the purpose and duration of their stay. This will help to avoid any delays or complications during the entry process.
- Not having a valid passport with at least six months' validity
- Not having proof of onward travel or sufficient funds to support stay
- Not completing the declaration form accurately or thoroughly
- Not declaring restricted items, such as plant or animal products
- Not having the required visa or documentation for the purpose of stay
- Not registering with the local authorities within 90 days of arrival (for long-term stays)
