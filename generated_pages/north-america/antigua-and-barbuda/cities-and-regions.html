How Antigua and Barbuda Is Divided

Antigua and Barbuda is a sovereign nation divided into 6 parishes and 2 dependencies. The parishes are Saint George, Saint John, Saint Mary, Saint Paul, Saint Peter, and Saint Philip, while the dependencies are the islands of Barbuda and Redonda. This administrative structure allows for local governance and decision-making, with the capital city of St. John's serving as the central hub for national administration. The parishes are further divided into smaller communities and villages, each with their own unique character and charm.

The division of Antigua and Barbuda into parishes and dependencies reflects the country's history and geography. The parishes were established by the British colonial administration, while the dependencies of Barbuda and Redonda are smaller islands that are part of the nation but have distinct cultural and ecological identities. This administrative structure has been maintained since independence in 1981 and continues to shape the country's governance and development.

Regional Differences

  • The parishes of Saint George and Saint John are the most urbanized and have a more developed infrastructure, with a focus on tourism and commerce.
  • The parishes of Saint Mary, Saint Paul, and Saint Peter are more rural and have a stronger focus on agriculture and natural resources.
  • The island of Barbuda has a distinct cultural and ecological identity, with a focus on conservation and community development.
  • The island of Redonda is uninhabited and is primarily used for conservation and scientific research.

Getting Around

Antigua and Barbuda has a well-developed transportation network, with a range of options for getting around the country. The international airport in St. John's is the main entry point for visitors, with flights connecting to major hubs in the Caribbean, North America, and Europe. Domestic flights are available between Antigua and Barbuda, with a flight time of around 15 minutes. The country has a comprehensive network of roads, with buses and taxis available for public transportation. Rental cars are also widely available, with driving conditions generally good. However, visitors should be aware that driving is on the left-hand side of the road, and road signs and markings may be different from those in their home country.

In addition to road transportation, Antigua and Barbuda has a range of water-based transportation options, including ferries and boats. The ferry between Antigua and Barbuda takes around 90 minutes, with multiple departures per day. Visitors can also take a boat tour to explore the country's stunning coastline and marine ecosystem. Overall, getting around Antigua and Barbuda is relatively easy, with a range of options available to suit different budgets and preferences.
