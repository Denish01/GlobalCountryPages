[SECTION] Healthcare Overview [/SECTION]
Nicaragua's healthcare system is a mix of public and private providers, with the public system being the primary source of care for the majority of the population. The public system is overseen by the Ministry of Health, which operates a network of hospitals, clinics, and health centers throughout the country. However, the public system is often underfunded and understaffed, which can result in long wait times and limited access to specialized care. Private healthcare providers, on the other hand, offer a higher level of care, but are generally more expensive and may not be accessible to the majority of the population. In urban areas, such as Managua, there are more healthcare options available, including private hospitals and clinics, while in rural areas, access to healthcare is often limited.

The quality of care in Nicaragua can vary significantly depending on the location and type of facility. Public hospitals often lack resources and equipment, and may not have access to specialized care or advanced medical technologies. Private hospitals, on the other hand, may have more modern equipment and better-trained staff, but can be expensive. In general, the quality of care in Nicaragua is lower than in many other countries in the region, and patients may need to travel to other countries, such as Costa Rica or the United States, for specialized care.

Despite these challenges, Nicaragua has made significant progress in improving healthcare outcomes in recent years, including reducing infant mortality rates and increasing access to healthcare services. However, more work needs to be done to address the systemic challenges facing the healthcare system, including inadequate funding, limited access to specialized care, and a shortage of trained healthcare professionals.

- **Local insurance options** are available, but may not provide the same level of coverage as international insurance plans.
- **Expats** may need to purchase private health insurance to access quality care, as the public system may not provide adequate coverage.
- **Costs** can vary depending on the type of insurance and the level of coverage, but can range from $50 to $500 per month.
- **International insurance plans** may be more expensive, but can provide access to a wider range of services and facilities.
- **Travel insurance** may also be recommended for short-term visitors, as it can provide emergency medical coverage and evacuation services.

[SECTION] Vaccinations & Health Risks [/SECTION]
- **Hepatitis A** vaccination is recommended for all travelers to Nicaragua.
- **Typhoid** vaccination is also recommended, especially for those who will be eating or drinking outside of major restaurants and hotels.
- **Malaria** is present in some areas of the country, and **malaria prophylaxis** may be recommended for travelers to high-risk areas.
- **Dengue fever** and **Zika virus** are also present in the country, and **insect repellent** and **protective clothing** are recommended to prevent mosquito-borne illnesses.
- **Water safety** is a concern, and travelers should avoid drinking tap water and stick to bottled or filtered water.
- **Food safety** is also a concern, and travelers should avoid eating undercooked meat or raw vegetables.
