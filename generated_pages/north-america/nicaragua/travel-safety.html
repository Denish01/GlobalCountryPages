Overall Safety Assessment

Nicaragua is a country with a complex safety landscape. While it is generally possible to have a safe and enjoyable trip, there are certain risks and precautions that travelers should be aware of. Petty crime, such as pickpocketing and theft, is a common problem in tourist areas, and violent crime can occur in certain parts of the country. Additionally, Nicaragua is prone to natural disasters, including earthquakes, hurricanes, and volcanic eruptions, which can pose a significant threat to travelers. However, with proper planning and precautions, many travelers are able to visit Nicaragua without incident.

It's worth noting that the country has experienced periods of civil unrest and protests in recent years, which have led to clashes between protesters and security forces. However, these events are typically limited to specific areas and can be avoided with proper planning and awareness. The Nicaraguan government has also taken steps to improve security in tourist areas, including the deployment of tourist police. Overall, while Nicaragua is not without its risks, it can be a safe and rewarding destination for travelers who are well-informed and take necessary precautions.

Common Risks & Scams

  • Be cautious of ATM scams, where thieves use cameras and skimmers to steal card information.
  • Be aware of pickpocketing in crowded areas, such as markets and bus stations.
  • Avoid displaying valuables, such as jewelry and expensive watches, which can attract unwanted attention.
  • Be cautious of unlicensed taxis, which can be a safety risk, especially at night.
  • Be aware of protests and demonstrations, which can turn violent and should be avoided.
  • Be cautious of credit card scams, where thieves use fake machines to steal card information.
  • Avoid carrying large amounts of cash, and use reputable currency exchange services instead.

Health & Medical

  • Vaccinations: Hepatitis A, typhoid, and rabies vaccinations are recommended for travel to Nicaragua.
  • Water Safety: Tap water is not safe to drink, and bottled or filtered water is recommended.
  • Medical Facilities: Medical facilities in Nicaragua are limited, especially outside of major cities, and medical evacuation may be necessary in serious cases.
  • Pharmacy Access: Pharmacies are widely available, but may not always have a wide selection of medications.

Practical Safety Tips

  • Always carry a copy of your passport and important documents, and leave the originals in a safe place.
  • Use reputable transportation services, such as licensed taxis and shuttle buses.
  • Avoid traveling at night, especially in rural areas, where roads can be poorly lit and crime rates are higher.
  • Stay informed about local conditions and events, and follow local advice and instructions.
  • Be respectful of local customs and traditions, and avoid displaying signs of wealth or affluence.
  • Learn some basic Spanish, which can help you communicate with locals and navigate the country more safely.
  • Stay hydrated and take regular breaks in the heat, especially when traveling in rural areas.
  • Use sunscreen and insect repellent, to protect against the sun and insects.

For Solo & Women Travelers

  • Be cautious of catcalls and harassment, which can be a problem in some areas, especially at night.
  • Dress modestly, and avoid displaying signs of wealth or affluence, which can attract unwanted attention.
  • Use reputable transportation services, and avoid hitchhiking or accepting rides from strangers.
  • Stay in touch with family and friends back home, and keep them informed of your itinerary and plans.
