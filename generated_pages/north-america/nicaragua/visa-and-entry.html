Visa Policy Overview

Nicaragua has a moderate visa policy, allowing citizens of many countries to enter without a visa or with a visa on arrival. The country welcomes tourists and business travelers, and its visa policy is designed to facilitate travel while maintaining national security. Citizens of over 90 countries can enter Nicaragua visa-free, including most European, North American, and Latin American nations.

The Nicaraguan government has implemented various visa types to cater to different types of travelers, including tourists, business travelers, students, and workers. The visa policy is subject to change, and it is essential to check with the official embassy or consulate of Nicaragua for the latest information. The country's visa policy aims to promote tourism, trade, and cultural exchange while ensuring the safety and security of its citizens and visitors.

By Visitor Type

Tourists: Tourists can enter Nicaragua visa-free for up to 90 days, with the possibility of extending their stay up to 6 months. They must provide proof of onward travel, sufficient funds, and health insurance. Typical stay for tourists is 7-14 days, and they can engage in various activities such as sightseeing, hiking, and cultural exchange.

Business Travelers: Business travelers may require a visa, depending on their nationality and the purpose of their visit. They must provide an invitation letter from a Nicaraguan company, proof of business activities, and other supporting documents. The requirements for business travelers differ from those for tourists, and they may need to register with the local authorities.

Students: Students must apply for a student visa, which requires proof of enrollment in a Nicaraguan educational institution, sufficient funds, and health insurance. The duration of the student visa depends on the length of the academic program.

Workers: Workers must obtain a work visa, which requires sponsorship from a Nicaraguan employer, proof of qualifications, and other supporting documents. The work visa process can be complex, and applicants must comply with Nicaraguan labor laws and regulations.
