[SECTION] Location & Borders [/SECTION]
San Francisco is situated on the western coast of the United States, in the state of California, and is surrounded by the Pacific Ocean to the west and the San Francisco Bay to the east. The city is bordered by the counties of Marin to the north, San Mateo to the south, and Alameda and Contra Costa to the east. San Francisco is also close to several other major cities, including Oakland and Berkeley, which are located across the bay.

The city's unique location on a peninsula, with the Pacific Ocean to the west and the San Francisco Bay to the east, has played a significant role in shaping its history, culture, and economy. The Golden Gate Strait, which connects the San Francisco Bay to the Pacific Ocean, is a major shipping channel and has been an important factor in the city's development as a major port. The city's proximity to the San Andreas Fault, a major fault line that runs through California, also makes it prone to earthquakes, which have had a significant impact on the city's architecture and emergency preparedness.

[SECTION] Terrain & Landscape [/SECTION]
San Francisco's terrain is characterized by steep hills, scenic valleys, and a rugged coastline. The city's landscape is dominated by the **Pacific Ocean**, which borders the city to the west, and the **San Francisco Bay**, which borders the city to the east. The city's hills, including **Nob Hill**, **Russian Hill**, and **Telegraph Hill**, are known for their steep slopes and scenic views. The city's valleys, including the **Mission Valley** and the **Haight Valley**, are home to many of the city's neighborhoods and commercial districts.

The city's coastline is also home to several **beaches**, including **Ocean Beach** and **Baker Beach**, which are popular with surfers and sunbathers. The **Golden Gate Park**, which is located in the western part of the city, is a large urban park that features several lakes, gardens, and museums. The park is also home to the **de Young Museum** and the **California Academy of Sciences**, which are two of the city's most popular cultural attractions.

The city's rugged terrain has also played a significant role in shaping its architecture and urban planning. Many of the city's buildings are designed to withstand earthquakes, and the city's famous **cable cars** are a testament to the city's steep hills and scenic valleys. The city's **neighborhoods**, including **Fisherman's Wharf**, **Chinatown**, and **Haight-Ashbury**, are also known for their unique character and charm.
