Greenland is a territory with a unique blend of traditional Inuit culture and modern Danish influences, boasting a range of notable achievements and positive highlights, from scientific research and innovative sustainability initiatives to vibrant arts and cultural traditions.

Notable Achievements

ScienceIlulissat Icefjord ResearchThe Ilulissat Icefjord, a UNESCO World Heritage site, is a key location for climate change research, with scientists from around the world studying the effects of global warming on the ice sheet
SportsArctic Circle MarathonThe Arctic Circle Marathon, held in Ilulissat, is one of the most challenging and unique marathons in the world, attracting runners from over 20 countries

Cultural Treasures

  • The Ilulissat Icefjord, a UNESCO World Heritage site, is a breathtaking example of Greenland's natural beauty and cultural significance.
  • The Qilaut throat singing tradition is an important part of Greenlandic culture and is often performed at cultural festivals and events.
  • The Tupilak art form, which involves creating intricate sculptures from whale bone and stone, is a unique and fascinating aspect of Greenlandic art.
  • The Kalaallit Nunaat national museum in Nuuk provides a comprehensive overview of Greenland's history, culture, and natural environment.
  • The Inuit Circumpolar Council is an international organization that promotes Inuit rights and cultural preservation, with a strong presence in Greenland.

People & Global Impact

  • Knud Rasmussen, a Danish-Greenlandic polar explorer and anthropologist, is considered one of the most important figures in Greenland's history and played a key role in promoting Inuit culture and rights.
  • The Inuit diaspora community in Denmark and other countries has made significant contributions to the preservation of Inuit culture and language.
  • Aaja Chemnitz Larsen, a Greenlandic politician and women's rights activist, has been a prominent figure in Greenlandic politics and has worked to promote gender equality and social justice.
  • The Greenlandic values of community, respect for nature, and resilience in the face of adversity are highly regarded and have been recognized internationally as an important part of the country's cultural identity.

What Visitors Love

  • The hospitality of the Greenlandic people is often cited as one of the highlights of visiting the country, with visitors praising the warm welcome and friendly nature of the locals.
  • The traditional Greenlandic cuisine, which includes dishes such as suaasat (a hearty meat stew) and mattak (whale skin with a layer of blubber), is a unique and flavorful experience.
  • The scenery in Greenland is breathtaking, with towering icebergs, majestic fjords, and stunning Northern Lights displays.
  • The dog sledding experience, which allows visitors to explore the Arctic landscape in a traditional and exhilarating way, is a popular and unforgettable experience.
  • The hiking trails in Greenland, which range from easy day hikes to more challenging multi-day treks, offer a unique opportunity to explore the country's vast and untouched wilderness.
  • The cultural festivals, such as the Nuuk Snow Festival and the Ilulissat Icefjord Festival, provide a vibrant and colorful showcase of Greenlandic music, dance, and art.

Hidden Gems

  • The Upernavik town, located in the north of Greenland, is a charming and picturesque community that offers a unique glimpse into traditional Greenlandic life.
  • The Eqi Glacier is a lesser-known but equally impressive glacier that offers stunning views and a range of outdoor activities, including hiking and kayaking.
  • The Tasiilaq town, located on the east coast of Greenland, is a hidden gem that offers a unique blend of traditional and modern culture, as well as breathtaking scenery and outdoor activities.
  • The Greenland National Museum's collection of Inuit artifacts and traditional clothing is a fascinating and often overlooked aspect of the museum's holdings, providing a unique insight into Greenland's rich cultural heritage.
