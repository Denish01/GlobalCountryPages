Location & Borders

Puerto Rico is situated in the northeastern Caribbean Sea, bordered by the Atlantic Ocean to the north and the Caribbean Sea to the south. The island is located east of the Dominican Republic and west of the US Virgin Islands, with the Mona Passage separating it from the Dominican Republic. The island's strategic location has made it an important hub for trade and commerce in the region. Puerto Rico is also part of the Greater Antilles archipelago, which includes other islands such as Cuba, Hispaniola, and Jamaica.

The island's borders are entirely composed of coastline, with a total length of approximately 700 kilometers. The Northeastern Trade Winds have a significant impact on the island's climate and geography, bringing warm, moist air from the tropics. The island's location in the tropical zone also means that it is susceptible to hurricanes, which can have a significant impact on the island's infrastructure and economy. The Tropic of Cancer passes through the northern part of the island, marking the boundary between the tropical and subtropical zones.

Terrain & Landscape

Puerto Rico's terrain is characterized by a mix of mountains, valleys, and coastal plains. The Central Mountain Range, which runs east-west through the center of the island, is home to the highest point, Cerro de Punta, which rises to an elevation of 1,338 meters. The Luquillo Mountains and the El Yunque National Forest are also notable features of the island's terrain, with the latter being the only tropical rainforest in the US National Forest System. The island's terrain is also home to numerous rivers, including the Rio Grande de Loiza and the Rio Guanajibo, which flow through the coastal plains and provide water for irrigation and other uses.

The island's coastline is marked by numerous beaches, bays, and coves, which provide a habitat for a diverse range of marine life. The northern coast is generally more rugged than the southern coast, with cliffs and rocky shores giving way to sandy beaches and mangrove swamps. The eastern coast is home to the El Yunque National Forest, which is a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve and provides a habitat for a wide range of plant and animal species. The island's terrain is also home to numerous waterfalls, including the La Mina Falls and the Juan Diego Creek Falls, which are popular tourist destinations.

The island's terrain has also been shaped by geological processes, including volcanic activity and erosion. The island is located near the boundary between the North American and Caribbean tectonic plates, which has resulted in the formation of numerous fault lines and volcanic features. The island's terrain is also home to numerous caves, including the Rio Camuy Cave Park, which is a popular tourist destination.
