[SECTION] What Is Bermuda? [/SECTION]
Bermuda is a British Overseas Territory located in the North Atlantic Ocean, situated approximately 640 miles east-southeast of Cape Hatteras, North Carolina, in the United States. The territory consists of seven main islands, with the largest being Grand Bermuda. Bermuda is known for its pink-sand beaches, clear turquoise waters, and coral reefs, making it a popular tourist destination. The territory has a unique blend of British and American influences, which is reflected in its culture, architecture, and daily life.

Geographically, Bermuda is an archipelago with a total area of approximately 21 square miles. The islands are volcanic in origin, with the highest point being Town Hill, which rises to about 250 feet above sea level. The climate is subtropical, with mild winters and warm summers, making it an attractive destination for visitors from around the world. Bermuda's economy is primarily driven by tourism, finance, and international business, with a high standard of living and a strong infrastructure.

Bermuda's sovereignty status is that of a British Overseas Territory, with the British monarch serving as the head of state. The territory has a significant degree of autonomy, with its own government and laws, but the United Kingdom is responsible for its defense and foreign affairs. This unique arrangement has allowed Bermuda to maintain its independence while still benefiting from its association with the United Kingdom.
