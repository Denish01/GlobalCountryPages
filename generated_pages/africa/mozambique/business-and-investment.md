# Doing Business in Mozambique — Investment, FDI & Opportunities

Doing business in Mozambique can be challenging, but the country offers significant investment opportunities in various sectors, particularly in natural resources, infrastructure, and agriculture.

[SECTION] Business Environment [/SECTION]
Mozambique's business environment is characterized by a mix of opportunities and challenges. The government has been actively promoting foreign investment, particularly in the extractive industries, and has established various agencies to support investors. However, the country still faces significant bureaucratic hurdles, and corruption remains a major concern. The government has implemented various reforms to improve the business climate, including the creation of a one-stop shop for investors and the simplification of tax procedures. Despite these efforts, the business environment remains complex, and investors often face difficulties in navigating the regulatory framework.

The government's attitude toward foreign investment is generally positive, and the country has signed various investment agreements with other nations to promote trade and investment. However, the level of bureaucracy remains high, and investors often face delays and inefficiencies in obtaining necessary permits and licenses. Corruption is also a significant challenge, and investors may face demands for bribes or other forms of corruption. To mitigate these risks, investors should ensure that they have a thorough understanding of the regulatory framework and work closely with local partners and advisors.

Mozambique's economy has significant potential for growth, particularly in the agriculture, mining, and manufacturing sectors. The country has abundant natural resources, including coal, gas, and minerals, and its strategic location makes it an important hub for trade in the region. However, the country still faces significant infrastructure challenges, including inadequate roads, ports, and energy systems. To address these challenges, the government has launched various initiatives to improve the country's infrastructure, including the development of new ports and transportation networks.

[SECTION] Legal & Regulatory Framework [/SECTION]
- The business laws in Mozambique are based on the Portuguese civil law system.
- Intellectual property protection is provided through the Intellectual Property Law, which offers protection for trademarks, patents, and copyrights.
- Labor laws are governed by the Labor Law, which provides for workers' rights and employment conditions.
- Dispute resolution is provided through the courts and arbitration tribunals.
- The government has established a one-stop shop for investors to facilitate the investment process.
- The tax authority is responsible for collecting taxes and providing tax incentives to investors.

[SECTION] Challenges & Risks [/SECTION]
- Corruption is a significant challenge, and investors may face demands for bribes or other forms of corruption.
- The business environment is complex, and investors may face difficulties in navigating the regulatory framework.
- Infrastructure challenges, including inadequate roads, ports, and energy systems, can increase costs and reduce efficiency.
- The currency can be volatile, and investors may face foreign exchange risks.
- The country is vulnerable to natural disasters, including floods and droughts, which can impact business operations.
- The government has implemented various measures to mitigate these risks, including the creation of a business ombudsman and the establishment of a corruption hotline.

- The government has established four special economic zones, including the Maputo Corridor and the Beira Corridor, which offer tax incentives and other benefits to investors.
- The tax authority offers various tax incentives, including tax breaks and investment subsidies, to attract FDI.
- The government has established investment promotion agencies, including the Investment Promotion Center, to facilitate the investment process and provide support to investors.
- The country is a member of various regional trade agreements, including the Southern African Development Community (SADC) and the Common Market for Eastern and Southern Africa (COMESA), which provide access to regional markets and offer tariff reductions and other benefits to investors.
