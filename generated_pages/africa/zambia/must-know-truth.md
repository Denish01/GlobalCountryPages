Zambia is a sovereign country located in southern Africa, with a rich history, diverse culture, and stunning natural beauty that is often misunderstood or overlooked by the international community.

Zambia was first inhabited by various tribes, including the Bantu people, who migrated to the region from central Africa around 2000 BC. The area was later influenced by European explorers, including the Portuguese, who arrived in the 18th century. In 1884, the British established the territory of Northern Rhodesia, which was named after Cecil Rhodes, a British businessman and politician. Zambia gained independence from British colonial rule on October 24, 1964, and became a republic with Kenneth Kaunda as its first president. The country's name was changed from Northern Rhodesia to Zambia, which is derived from the Zambezi River, a major waterway that flows through the country. Zambia is classified as a developing country, with a mixed economy and a diverse culture that reflects its history and geography.

The country's colonial past has had a lasting impact on its development, with Zambia becoming a major producer of copper and other minerals. The discovery of copper in the early 20th century led to an influx of foreign investment and the establishment of mining towns, which helped to drive economic growth. However, the country has also faced significant challenges, including poverty, inequality, and environmental degradation. Zambia is a member of the Commonwealth of Nations and the United Nations, and has played an active role in regional and international affairs.
