Economic Overview

Zambia is a sovereign country located in southern Africa, with a diverse economy that has undergone significant transformations since its independence in 1964. The country has a lower-middle-income economy, with a GDP per capita of around $1,143, and an unemployment rate of approximately 12%. Zambia's economic classification is lower-middle, indicating a developing economy with a mix of traditional and modern sectors. The country's development level is also characterized by a growing service sector, which accounts for the largest share of the economy. Zambia's economic trajectory has been shaped by its natural resource endowment, particularly copper, which has been a major driver of the economy for decades.

Despite the dominance of copper, Zambia has been working to diversify its economy, with a focus on agriculture, tourism, and manufacturing. The government has implemented various initiatives to promote economic growth and development, including investment in infrastructure, trade facilitation, and business environment reforms. Zambia's economy has also been impacted by external factors, such as fluctuations in global commodity prices and climate change, which have affected the country's agricultural sector. The country's economic growth has been moderate, with an average annual growth rate of around 3-4% over the past decade.

The Zambian government has recognized the need to accelerate economic growth and development, and has set out a vision to become a middle-income country by 2030. To achieve this goal, the government has identified key priority areas, including economic diversification, job creation, and poverty reduction. The government has also emphasized the importance of private sector development, and has implemented various initiatives to promote entrepreneurship and investment. These efforts aim to create a more diversified and resilient economy, with a stronger focus on value addition and export-oriented industries.

  • Fuel and petroleum products, which are critical for the country's energy needs
  • Vehicles, which are in high demand for both personal and commercial use

  • Pharmaceuticals, which are essential for the country's healthcare sector

Infrastructure

  • Transport networks: Zambia has a well-developed road network, with over 40,000 kilometers of roads, as well as a rail network that connects the country to its neighbors. The country also has several major airports, including Kenneth Kaunda International Airport in Lusaka.
  • Energy: Zambia has significant hydroelectric power potential, with the Kariba Dam being one of the largest hydroelectric dams in the world. The country is also exploring other energy sources, including solar and wind power.
  • Internet and mobile penetration: Zambia has made significant progress in expanding its telecommunications network, with over 80% of the population having access to mobile phones and around 30% having access to the internet.
  • Ports and airports: Zambia has several major ports, including the Port of Ndola, which is a key hub for trade with neighboring countries. The country also has several international airports, including Harry Mwanga Nkumbula International Airport in Livingstone.

Economic Outlook

Zambia's economic outlook is positive, with the country expected to maintain a moderate growth rate of around 4-5% per annum over the next few years. The government has identified key priority areas, including economic diversification, job creation, and poverty reduction, and has implemented various initiatives to promote economic growth and development. The country is also expected to benefit from significant investment in infrastructure, including the construction of new roads, railways, and energy projects. However, Zambia's economy is also vulnerable to external shocks, including fluctuations in global commodity prices and climate change, which could impact the country's agricultural sector.

The Zambian government has recognized the need to accelerate economic growth and development, and has set out a vision to become a middle-income country by 2030. To achieve this goal, the government has identified key priority areas, including economic diversification, job creation, and poverty reduction. The government has also emphasized the importance of private sector development, and has implemented various initiatives to promote entrepreneurship and investment. These efforts aim to create a more diversified and resilient economy, with a stronger focus on value addition and export-oriented industries. Major projects and reforms include the development of the Kafue Gorge Lower Hydroelectric Power Project, which is expected to increase the country's power generation capacity, and the implementation of the Seventh National Development Plan, which aims to promote economic growth and development over the next five years.
