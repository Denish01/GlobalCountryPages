# Ethiopia History — Key Events & Timeline

The history of Ethiopia is a rich and diverse tapestry spanning over 3,000 years, from the ancient kingdoms of D'mt and Axum to the modern-day federal democratic republic.

[SECTION] Historical Overview [/SECTION]
Ethiopia's history began with the emergence of the **D'mt** kingdom in the 10th century BC, which was later replaced by the **Axum** kingdom in the 1st century AD. Axum was a major power in the region, known for its **obelisks**, **coinage**, and **trade networks**. The kingdom of Axum declined in the 7th century AD, and Ethiopia was later divided into several smaller kingdoms. In the 13th century, the **Solomonic dynasty** was established, which claimed descent from the biblical King Solomon and the Queen of Sheba. This dynasty ruled Ethiopia for over 700 years, with some notable exceptions, and played a significant role in shaping the country's **language**, **culture**, and **religion**. In the 19th century, Ethiopia was subject to **colonial pressures** from European powers, particularly Italy, but it maintained its independence and became a symbol of African resistance against colonialism.

In the 20th century, Ethiopia underwent significant changes, including the **Italian occupation** from 1936 to 1941, and the subsequent **reign of Haile Selassie**, who modernized the country and played a key role in the formation of the **African Union**. However, Haile Selassie's regime was overthrown in 1974, and Ethiopia was ruled by a **Marxist-Leninist military junta** known as the **Derg**. The Derg's rule was marked by **repression**, **famine**, and **civil war**, which lasted for over 15 years. In 1991, the Derg was overthrown, and Ethiopia transitioned to a **federal democratic republic**, with a **constitution** that guaranteed **human rights** and **regional autonomy**.

The modern era has seen significant economic growth and development in Ethiopia, with a focus on **agriculture**, **industry**, and **infrastructure**. However, the country still faces challenges related to **poverty**, **inequality**, and **conflict**, particularly in the **Tigray region**. Despite these challenges, Ethiopia remains a **regional power** and a **symbol of African resilience** and **determination**. The country's rich cultural heritage, including its **music**, **art**, and **literature**, continues to inspire and influence people around the world.

Ethiopia's history is also marked by its **unique cultural practices**, such as the **Ethiopian Orthodox Church**, which is one of the oldest Christian churches in the world. The country's **calendar**, **script**, and **language** are also distinct and reflect its rich cultural heritage. Overall, Ethiopia's history is a complex and fascinating tapestry that reflects the country's strategic location, cultural diversity, and resilience in the face of adversity.

[SECTION] Founding & Formation [/SECTION]
Ethiopia's founding and formation are closely tied to its **legendary history**, which dates back to the **Queen of Sheba** and the **biblical King Solomon**. According to legend, the Queen of Sheba traveled to Jerusalem to meet King Solomon, and their union produced a son, **Menelik I**, who went on to found the Solomonic dynasty. This dynasty ruled Ethiopia for over 700 years, shaping the country's **language**, **culture**, and **religion**. The Solomonic dynasty played a significant role in shaping the country's **identity** and **heritage**.

The modern Ethiopian state was formed in the late 19th century, when **Emperor Menelik II** united various regions and kingdoms under his rule. Menelik II played a key role in shaping the country's **borders**, **administration**, and **economy**. He also introduced a series of **modernizing reforms**, including the establishment of a **modern education system**, a **national bank**, and a **telegraph network**. Menelik II's reforms had a significant impact on the country and paved the way for its **independence** and **development**.

[SECTION] Colonial & Independence Period [/SECTION]
Ethiopia was subject to **colonial pressures** from European powers, particularly Italy, in the late 19th century. In 1889, the **Treaty of Wuchale** was signed, which marked the beginning of Italian colonial influence in Ethiopia. However, the treaty was **rejected** by the Ethiopian people, and the **First Italo-Ethiopian War** broke out in 1895. The war ended with the **Battle of Adwa** in 1896, in which Ethiopian forces defeated the Italian army and maintained the country's independence.

The **Italian occupation** of Ethiopia lasted from 1936 to 1941, during which time the country was subject to **repression**, **exploitation**, and **cultural suppression**. However, the Ethiopian people **resisted** the occupation, and the **patriotic movement** played a key role in the country's **liberation**. The liberation was achieved with the help of **British forces**, which defeated the Italian army in 1941. Haile Selassie returned to power and introduced a series of **modernizing reforms**, including the establishment of a **modern education system**, a **national bank**, and a **telegraph network**.
