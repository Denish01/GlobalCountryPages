# South Sudan vs Sudan

When deciding between South Sudan and Sudan, consider factors such as safety, culture, and travel ease to make an informed decision that suits your needs.

[SECTION] Why People Compare Them [/SECTION]
The comparison between South Sudan and Sudan is a common one due to their shared history and geographic proximity. Until 2011, the two countries were a single entity, with South Sudan gaining independence after a long civil war. As a result, they share many cultural and linguistic ties, but have also developed distinct differences in terms of politics, economy, and tourism infrastructure. This shared history and proximity make it natural for travelers and investors to compare and contrast the two countries when planning a trip or making business decisions.

[SECTION] Key Differences [/SECTION]
- **Population**: South Sudan has a significantly smaller population compared to Sudan, with approximately 15 million people compared to Sudan's 43 million.
- **Size**: Sudan is roughly three times larger than South Sudan in terms of land area, covering over 1.8 million square kilometers.
- **Language**: While English is an official language in both countries, Arabic is more widely spoken in Sudan, reflecting its stronger cultural and historical ties to the Arab world.
- **Currency**: The two countries have distinct currencies, with South Sudan using the South Sudanese Pound (SSP) and Sudan using the Sudanese Pound (SDG).
- **Climate**: South Sudan has a tropical climate, while Sudan's climate varies from desert in the north to tropical in the south.
- **Safety**: South Sudan is generally considered less safe than Sudan due to ongoing conflict and instability, particularly outside the capital city of Juba.

[SECTION] Key Similarities [/SECTION]
- **Cultural Heritage**: Both countries share a rich cultural heritage, with many ethnic groups and traditional practices that are similar or identical.
- **Natural Resources**: Both South Sudan and Sudan are rich in natural resources, including oil, gas, and minerals, which has been a significant factor in their economies.
- **Agriculture**: Agriculture is a crucial sector in both countries, with crops such as cotton, sugarcane, and sesame being major exports.
- **Wildlife**: Both countries are home to diverse and abundant wildlife, including elephants, lions, and giraffes, making them attractive destinations for safari tourism.
