Location & Borders

Mayotte is situated in the Comoros archipelago, which is a group of islands located in the Indian Ocean, off the coast of East Africa. The territory is bordered by the Comoros to the northwest and Madagascar to the southeast. Mayotte is surrounded by the Indian Ocean, which provides a significant source of income for the territory through fishing and tourism. The island's strategic location in the Indian Ocean has made it an important stopover for ships traveling between Europe and Asia.

The territory's borders are primarily maritime, with the exception of the border with the Comoros, which is a neighboring island nation. Mayotte's location in the Indian Ocean has made it vulnerable to the impacts of climate change, including rising sea levels and increased storm activity. The territory's government has implemented measures to mitigate the effects of climate change, including the construction of sea walls and the promotion of sustainable tourism practices.

Terrain & Landscape

Mayotte's terrain is characterized by a mix of volcanic mountains, coastal plains, and coral reefs. The island's highest point, Benara, is a volcanic peak that rises to an elevation of 660 meters. The island's landscape is also dotted with numerous hills and valleys, which are covered in a mix of tropical forests and grasslands. The coastal plains are narrow and are lined with coral reefs, which provide a habitat for a diverse array of marine life.

The island's interior is home to a number of rivers, including the Sambi and the Mroala, which flow into the Indian Ocean. The rivers are an important source of freshwater for the island's inhabitants and are also used for irrigation and hydroelectric power. Mayotte's coastline is approximately 185 kilometers long and is characterized by a mix of sandy beaches, rocky shores, and coral reefs. The coastline is an important part of the island's economy, with many tourists visiting the island to enjoy its beautiful beaches and coral reefs.

The island's volcanic origin has created a unique landscape, with numerous volcanic peaks and valleys. The volcanic soil is fertile and supports a wide range of crops, including vanilla, coffee, and cloves. The island's agriculture is an important part of its economy, with many farmers growing crops for both domestic consumption and export.
