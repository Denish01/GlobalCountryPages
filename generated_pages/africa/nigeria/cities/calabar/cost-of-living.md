# Cost of Living in Calabar - Prices & Budget Guide

To live in or visit Calabar, you can expect to spend around $20-50 USD per day as a backpacker, $50-100 USD per day as a mid-range traveler, and $100-200 USD per day for a more comfortable experience.

Daily Budget (Comfort): $100-200 USD
Currency: NGN

[/FACTBOX]

[SECTION] How Expensive Is Calabar? [/SECTION]
Calabar, a city located in the southeastern part of Nigeria, is considered an affordable destination compared to other major cities in Africa. The cost of living in Calabar is relatively low, making it an attractive option for travelers and expats. Compared to its neighboring cities, Calabar has a lower cost of living, with affordable accommodation options, food, and transportation. However, prices can vary depending on the location, with the city center tend to be more expensive than the outskirts.

The cost of living in Calabar is also influenced by the local economy, with the city being a major hub for trade and commerce in the region. The city's economy is driven by industries such as manufacturing, agriculture, and tourism, which provides a range of employment opportunities for locals and expats alike. Overall, Calabar offers a great balance of affordability and quality of life, making it an excellent choice for those looking to experience the best of Nigerian culture without breaking the bank.

[SECTION] Money-Saving Tips [/SECTION]
- Consider staying in a hostel or guesthouse instead of a hotel to save on accommodation costs.
- Eat at local eateries or street food stalls to experience authentic Nigerian cuisine at a lower cost.
- Use public transportation or walk/bike whenever possible to save on transport costs.
- Shop at local markets or supermarkets for groceries and other essentials instead of relying on convenience stores or tourist-oriented shops.
- Avoid exchanging money at airports or hotels, as the rates tend to be unfavorable - instead, use ATMs or local banks to withdraw cash.
- Learn some basic Nigerian phrases, such as "hello" (kóyo) and "thank you" (ìmèélé), to show respect for the local culture and potentially receive better service or prices from vendors and locals.
