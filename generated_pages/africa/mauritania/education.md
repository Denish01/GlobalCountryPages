[SECTION] Education System Overview [/SECTION]
The education system in Mauritania is structured into three main levels: primary, secondary, and tertiary education. Primary education is compulsory for children between the ages of 6 and 12 and is free for all citizens. The primary education curriculum focuses on Arabic language, mathematics, science, and Islamic studies. Secondary education is divided into two cycles: the first cycle lasts for 4 years and the second cycle lasts for 3 years. Students who complete secondary education can pursue tertiary education at one of the country's universities or institutions. The quality of education in Mauritania varies between public and private schools, with private schools generally offering better facilities and more qualified teachers.

The public education system in Mauritania faces several challenges, including a lack of resources, inadequate infrastructure, and a shortage of qualified teachers. Despite these challenges, the government has implemented various reforms to improve the education sector, including the introduction of new curricula and the establishment of vocational training programs. The private education sector in Mauritania is growing rapidly, with many private schools and universities offering a range of academic programs.

The education system in Mauritania is also influenced by Islamic traditions and values, with many schools and universities offering courses in Islamic studies and Arabic language. The country's education system is also shaped by its cultural and historical context, with a strong emphasis on preserving traditional values and customs.

[SECTION] Education Quality & Challenges [/SECTION]
- The education system in Mauritania faces several challenges, including a lack of **qualified teachers**, inadequate **infrastructure**, and limited **resources**.
- The country has a high **dropout rate**, especially among girls and students from rural areas.
- The education system in Mauritania is also criticized for its emphasis on **rote learning** and lack of **critical thinking** skills.
- Despite these challenges, the government has implemented various **reforms** to improve the education sector, including the introduction of new **curricula** and the establishment of **vocational training programs**.
- The country has also made significant progress in increasing **enrollment rates**, especially at the primary level, with a **net enrollment rate** of over 70%.
