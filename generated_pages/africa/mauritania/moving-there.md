# Moving to Mauritania — Visas, Residency & Expat Guide

To move to Mauritania, you will need to navigate the country's complex visa and residency requirements, understand the local culture and customs, and be prepared for the challenges that come with living in a developing country in West Africa.

[SECTION] Why People Move to Mauritania [/SECTION]
Mauritania is a country located in the Maghreb region of West Africa, with a diverse culture and a growing economy. While it may not be a typical expat destination, some people are drawn to the country's unique blend of African, Arab, and French influences. The majority of expats in Mauritania are professionals working in the mining, fishing, or humanitarian sectors, as well as diplomats and aid workers. The quality of life in Mauritania can be challenging, with high temperatures, limited infrastructure, and a conservative social environment. However, for those who are adventurous and flexible, Mauritania can offer a unique and rewarding experience.

The expat community in Mauritania is relatively small, with most foreigners living in the capital city of Nouakchott. The community is diverse, with people from various countries and backgrounds, and there are some social and cultural activities available, such as sports clubs, cultural events, and volunteer opportunities. Despite the challenges, many expats find that the warm hospitality and welcoming nature of the Mauritanian people make it a pleasant place to live.

Mauritania has a lot to offer, from its stunning natural beauty, including the Sahara Desert and the Atlantic coast, to its rich cultural heritage, including historic towns, museums, and festivals. The country is also home to a diverse range of wildlife, including dolphins, whales, and birds. For those who are interested in outdoor activities, such as hiking, fishing, or camping, Mauritania can be a great destination.

[SECTION] Banking & Finances [/SECTION]
- Opening a bank account in Mauritania can be challenging, but some banks, such as the Banque Centrale de Mauritanie and the Banque Mauritanienne pour le Commerce International, offer services for foreigners.
- Money transfers can be done through Western Union or other international money transfer services, but the fees can be high.
- The local currency is the Mauritanian ouguiya, but euros and US dollars are widely accepted.
- Credit cards are not widely accepted, and ATMs may not always have cash, so it's essential to have some local currency with you.
- The cost of living in Mauritania is relatively low, but the prices of some imported goods can be high due to the country's limited infrastructure and high transportation costs.

[SECTION] Challenges & Honest Advice [/SECTION]
- Culture shock can be significant in Mauritania, where the customs and traditions are very different from those in Western countries.
- The bureaucracy in Mauritania can be slow and frustrating, and it's essential to be patient and persistent when dealing with official matters.
- Language barriers can be a challenge, as Arabic is the official language, and not everyone speaks French or English.
- The heat and dust in Mauritania can be overwhelming, especially during the summer months.
- The lack of infrastructure and limited access to some services, such as healthcare and education, can be a challenge for expats.
