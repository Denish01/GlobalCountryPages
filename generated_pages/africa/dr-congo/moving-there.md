# Moving to DR Congo — Visas, Residency & Expat Guide

To move to DR Congo, you will need to carefully plan and prepare for the challenges of relocating to a country with a complex and often unpredictable environment, while also being open to the unique cultural and economic opportunities that it presents.

[SECTION] Why People Move to DR Congo [/SECTION]
The Democratic Republic of Congo is a vast and diverse country, with a rich cultural heritage and abundant natural resources, attracting expats and foreigners for various reasons, including **mining and extraction** opportunities, **humanitarian work**, and **business investments**. The majority of expats in DR Congo are from European countries, such as France and Belgium, as well as from other African nations, and they are often drawn to the country's major cities, including Kinshasa and Lubumbashi. Despite the challenges of living in DR Congo, many expats find the country to be a fascinating and rewarding place to live, with a unique blend of traditional and modern culture, and a **vibrant music and art scene**.

The quality of life in DR Congo can be quite high for expats, with modern amenities and services available in the major cities, although the cost of living can be relatively high, especially for imported goods and luxury items. However, the country's **infrastructure and security** can be a concern, especially outside of the major cities, and expats need to take necessary precautions to stay safe and healthy. Overall, living in DR Congo can be a rich and rewarding experience, but it requires careful planning, flexibility, and a willingness to adapt to the local culture and way of life.

The expat community in DR Congo is relatively small, but it is diverse and active, with many social and cultural events and activities available, including **expat clubs and organizations**, **volunteer opportunities**, and **cultural festivals**. Expats can also connect with each other through online forums and social media groups, which can be a valuable resource for information and support.

[SECTION] Expat Communities [/SECTION]
- Kinshasa has a large and diverse expat community, with many social and cultural events and activities available.
- Lubumbashi is another major city with a significant expat population, with a more laid-back and relaxed atmosphere.
- The expat community in DR Congo is active on social media, with many online forums and groups available for information and support.
- The **American International School of Kinshasa** is a popular school for expat children, offering an American-style curriculum.
- The **British Embassy** in Kinshasa also offers support and services for British expats, including consular assistance and events.

[SECTION] Banking & Finances [/SECTION]
- Opening a bank account in DR Congo can be a complex process, requiring a residence permit and proof of income.
- Money transfers can be made through international banks and money transfer services, but be aware of high fees and exchange rates.
- The **Congolese franc** is the local currency, but US dollars and euros are also widely accepted.
- Credit cards are not widely accepted in DR Congo, so it's essential to have cash and a debit card.
- **Mobile payment systems** are becoming increasingly popular in DR Congo, offering a convenient and secure way to make transactions.

[SECTION] Challenges & Honest Advice [/SECTION]
- **Culture shock** can be a significant challenge for expats in DR Congo, with a very different way of life and cultural norms.
- **Bureaucracy** can be slow and frustrating, with complex and often unclear procedures.
- **Language barriers** can be a problem, especially outside of the major cities, where French and local languages are more widely spoken.
- **Security concerns** are a major issue in DR Congo, with a high risk of crime and instability in some areas.
- **Health concerns** are also a significant challenge, with a high risk of diseases such as malaria and Ebola.
- **Infrastructure challenges** can make everyday life difficult, with frequent power outages and limited access to basic services.
