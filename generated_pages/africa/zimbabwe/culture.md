# Zimbabwe Culture - Food, Traditions & Etiquette

Zimbabwe's culture is a rich and diverse blend of traditional African, European, and Asian influences, shaped by its complex history, ethnic diversity, and stunning natural environment.

[SECTION] Cultural Identity [/SECTION]
Zimbabwe's cultural identity is defined by its ethnic and linguistic diversity, with the majority of the population belonging to the **Shona** and **Ndebele** ethnic groups. The country has a total of 16 languages, with English, Shona, and Ndebele being the most widely spoken. The cultural landscape is also influenced by the country's history, with evidence of ancient civilizations such as **Great Zimbabwe** and **Khami**. The country's strategic location at the crossroads of southern Africa has also led to the influx of various cultures, including European, Asian, and other African cultures.

The cultural identity of Zimbabwe is also reflected in its traditional customs and practices, such as the importance of **respect for elders** and the **extended family** system. The country's cultural heritage is also preserved through its traditional music, dance, and art, which are often used to tell stories and pass down history and values from one generation to the next. The **Shona** people, for example, are known for their skilled craftsmanship, including woodcarvings, stone sculptures, and basket weaving.

The cultural diversity of Zimbabwe is also evident in its urban centers, where modern and traditional cultures blend together. The capital city, **Harare**, is a hub of cultural activity, with a vibrant arts scene, numerous museums, and a variety of cultural festivals and events throughout the year. The city is also home to a diverse population, with people from different ethnic and cultural backgrounds living and working together.

The country is also known for its exotic meats, such as **warthog** and **impala**, which are often served at special occasions.

[SECTION] Etiquette: Do's and Don'ts [/SECTION]
**Do:**
- Respect for elders is highly valued in Zimbabwean culture, so be sure to greet older individuals with a handshake or a bow
- Learn a few basic phrases in Shona or Ndebele, such as **"hello"** (shibobo) or **"thank you"** (tatisi)
- Remove your shoes when entering a traditional Zimbabwean home or cultural site
- Use your right hand when giving or receiving something, as the left hand is considered unclean
- Be prepared to engage in conversation and share stories, as Zimbabweans value social interaction and community
- Respect local customs and traditions, especially when attending cultural events or visiting sacred sites

**Don't:**
- Avoid public displays of affection, as they are generally frowned upon in Zimbabwean culture
- Refrain from using your left hand when eating or interacting with others
- Do not tip excessively, as it can be seen as an insult or a sign of disrespect
- Avoid taking pictures of people or cultural sites without permission, as it can be considered rude or disrespectful
- Do not litter or dispose of waste inappropriately, as Zimbabweans take pride in their natural environment
- Avoid discussing sensitive topics, such as politics or ethnicity, as they can be divisive and uncomfortable

[SECTION] Arts & Music [/SECTION]
- **Mbira music**: a traditional Zimbabwean instrument and music style, characterized by its distinctive sound and complex rhythms
- **Shona sculpture**: a traditional art form that involves carving and sculpting stone, wood, or other materials to create intricate and beautiful pieces
- **Ndebele beadwork**: a traditional craft that involves creating intricate and colorful beadwork patterns, often used to adorn clothing and accessories
- **Zimbabwean literature**: a growing literary scene, with notable authors such as **Dambudzo Marechera** and **Yvonne Vera**
- **Chimurenga music**: a genre of music that emerged during the country's struggle for independence, characterized by its powerful and emotive sound
