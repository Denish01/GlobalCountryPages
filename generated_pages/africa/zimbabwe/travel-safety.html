Overall Safety Assessment

Zimbabwe is a country with a rich cultural heritage and breathtaking natural beauty, but it also faces significant economic and social challenges that can impact tourist safety. While most visitors have a trouble-free experience, there are areas where crime and scams are more prevalent, and tourists should be cautious when visiting crowded areas, using public transportation, or engaging with strangers. The country has a high unemployment rate, which can contribute to petty crime, and the economic situation can lead to power outages, water shortages, and other infrastructure challenges.

However, Zimbabwe has a strong tradition of hospitality, and locals are generally friendly and welcoming to tourists. The country has a well-developed tourist infrastructure, with many reputable tour operators, lodges, and hotels that cater to international visitors. With proper planning, research, and precautions, tourists can have a safe and enjoyable experience in Zimbabwe. It's essential to stay informed about local conditions, respect local customs and traditions, and take necessary precautions to protect yourself and your belongings.

Common Risks & Scams

  • Be cautious of petty theft and pickpocketing in crowded areas, such as markets and bus stations.
  • Avoid carrying large amounts of cash and use reputable currency exchange services.
  • Be wary of scams involving ATM skimming and credit card fraud.
  • Be cautious of overcharging by taxi drivers and unofficial tour guides.
  • Avoid wildlife poaching and smuggling activities, which can be dangerous and illegal.
  • Be aware of protests and demonstrations, which can turn violent, and avoid areas where they are taking place.

Health & Medical

  • Vaccinations: Recommended vaccinations include hepatitis A, typhoid, and rabies, while yellow fever vaccination is required for travelers coming from or passing through yellow fever-endemic countries.
  • Water Safety: Tap water is not safe to drink, and bottled or filtered water is recommended.
  • Medical Facilities: Medical facilities are available in major cities, but the quality of care can vary, and medical evacuation to South Africa or other countries may be necessary in serious cases.
  • Pharmacy Access: Pharmacy access is limited in rural areas, and it's essential to bring necessary medications and supplies.

Practical Safety Tips

  • Research and plan your trip carefully, and stay informed about local conditions.
  • Use reputable tour operators and guides to minimize risks.
  • Avoid traveling at night, especially in rural areas, and use secure transportation.
  • Keep valuables secure and be mindful of your surroundings in crowded areas.
  • Respect local customs and traditions, and avoid engaging in activities that may be perceived as disrespectful.
  • Stay hydrated and bring necessary medications, especially when traveling to remote areas.
  • Learn some basic Shona or Ndebele phrases to show respect for the local culture.

For Solo & Women Travelers

  • Be cautious of harassment and catcalls, especially in crowded areas, and avoid walking alone at night.
  • Dress modestly and respect local customs, especially when visiting rural areas or attending cultural events.
  • Use reputable transportation services, such as taxis or shuttle services, and avoid hitchhiking or accepting rides from strangers.
  • Stay in touch with family and friends back home, and consider registering with your government's travel advisory program to receive important safety and security updates.
