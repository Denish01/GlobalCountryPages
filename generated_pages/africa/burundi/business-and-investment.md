# Doing Business in Burundi — Investment, FDI & Opportunities

Doing business in Burundi can be challenging due to its complex regulatory environment and limited infrastructure, but the country also offers investment opportunities in key sectors such as agriculture, mining, and energy.

[SECTION] Business Environment [/SECTION]
The business environment in Burundi is characterized by a complex and often unpredictable regulatory framework, which can make it difficult for foreign investors to navigate. The government has taken steps to improve the business climate, including the creation of a one-stop shop for business registration and the establishment of a investment promotion agency. However, corruption remains a significant challenge, and companies may face demands for bribes or other forms of extortion. The government's attitude towards foreign investment is generally positive, but the country's political instability and security concerns can deter some investors.

The bureaucracy level in Burundi is relatively high, with many procedures requiring multiple steps and approvals from different government agencies. This can lead to delays and increased costs for businesses. Additionally, the country's infrastructure is limited, with poor roads, limited access to electricity, and inadequate telecommunications services. Despite these challenges, Burundi has significant potential for economic growth, driven by its strategic location, natural resources, and young population.

The government has implemented some reforms aimed at improving the business climate, including the simplification of tax procedures and the creation of a business ombudsman to help resolve disputes. However, more needs to be done to address the underlying challenges and create a more favorable environment for investment.

- FDI inflows to Burundi have been increasing in recent years, driven by investments in the agriculture, mining, and energy sectors

[SECTION] Legal & Regulatory Framework [/SECTION]
- The business law in Burundi is based on the French civil code and the **OHADA** (Organization for the Harmonization of Business Law in Africa) uniform acts
- Intellectual property protection is provided by the **TRIPS** (Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights) agreement and the **Paris Convention**
- Labor laws in Burundi are based on the **ILO** (International Labor Organization) conventions and the labor code
- Dispute resolution is provided by the courts and arbitration tribunals
- The government has established a business ombudsman to help resolve disputes and improve the business climate
- The legal framework is still evolving, and investors should seek professional advice to ensure compliance with all regulations

[SECTION] Challenges & Risks [/SECTION]
- **Political risk**: Burundi has experienced political instability and security concerns in recent years, which can deter foreign investment
- **Corruption**: Corruption is a significant challenge in Burundi, and companies may face demands for bribes or other forms of extortion
- **Infrastructure**: The country's infrastructure is limited, with poor roads, limited access to electricity, and inadequate telecommunications services
- **Market limitations**: The domestic market is small, and companies may need to rely on exports to achieve economies of scale
- **Currency risks**: The Burundian franc is a volatile currency, and companies may face exchange rate risks
- **Regulatory risks**: The regulatory framework is still evolving, and companies may face changes in laws and regulations that can affect their operations
