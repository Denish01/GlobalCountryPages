[SECTION] Getting Around Overview [/SECTION]
Somalia has a relatively underdeveloped transportation system, with most people relying on **minibuses**, **taxis**, and **private vehicles** to get around. The quality of public transit is generally poor, with limited coverage and frequent delays. However, the country's **port cities**, such as Mogadishu and Kismayo, have a more developed transportation network, with a greater variety of options available. In these cities, **buses** and **minibuses** are the primary mode of public transportation, while **taxis** are also widely available.

In rural areas, **donkey carts** and **camel rides** are still common modes of transportation, particularly for short distances. The lack of a well-developed transportation infrastructure makes it challenging to travel long distances, and **road conditions** can be poor, especially during the rainy season. Despite these challenges, the Somali government has been working to improve the transportation network, with plans to **rebuild roads** and **expand public transportation** in the coming years.

[SECTION] Transport Tips [/SECTION]
- To save money on transportation, consider using **minibuses** or **shared taxis**, which are often cheaper than **private taxis**.
- When using **taxis**, it's essential to **negotiate the fare** beforehand, as meters are not always available.
- For long-distance travel, **booking in advance** can help secure a seat on **buses** or **flights**, which can be limited.
- **Road safety** is a concern in Somalia, particularly at night, so it's recommended to **travel with a reputable company** or **use a trusted driver**.
- **Local knowledge** is essential when navigating Somalia's transportation network, so consider **hiring a guide** or **using a local app** to help you get around.
- **Be prepared for delays** and **flexible schedules**, as the transportation network in Somalia can be unpredictable.
