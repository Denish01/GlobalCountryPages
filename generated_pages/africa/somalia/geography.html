Location & Borders

Somalia is located in the eastern part of Africa, commonly referred to as the Horn of Africa. It is situated on the coastline of the Indian Ocean and borders three countries: Djibouti to the northwest, Ethiopia to the west, and Kenya to the southwest. The country's strategic location allows it to control the southern entrance to the Bab-el-Mandeb strait, which connects the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden. Somalia's coastline stretches for over 3,300 kilometers, providing ample access to the Indian Ocean and its rich marine resources.

The country's borders have been a subject of contention over the years, with disputes arising from the colonial-era demarcations. The border with Ethiopia, in particular, has been a source of tension, with both countries claiming ownership of the Ogaden region. Despite these challenges, Somalia's location has also facilitated trade and cultural exchange with its neighbors and the wider world. The country's ports, such as Mogadishu and Kismayo, have long been important centers of commerce, connecting the region to the global economy.

Terrain & Landscape

Somalia's terrain is characterized by a mix of mountains, deserts, and coastal plains. The northern part of the country is dominated by the Ogo Mountains, which stretch from the Ethiopian border to the Gulf of Aden. The highest point in the country, Shimbiris, is located in this range. The central and southern regions are mostly flat, with the Haud plateau covering much of the area. The Haud is a vast, arid region that is home to numerous wadis, or dry riverbeds, which fill with water during the rainy season.

The coastal plains, which stretch along the Indian Ocean, are generally narrow and flat, with numerous beaches and coves. The Juba River and the Shabelle River are the two main rivers in the country, flowing through the southern region and providing water for irrigation and drinking. Somalia's landscape is also dotted with acacia trees and other thorny vegetation, which are well adapted to the country's arid climate. The absence of dense forests and the limited agricultural land make Somalia's environment challenging for human habitation and economic development.

The country's desert regions, including the Chalbi Desert and the Migiurtinia, cover much of the northern and central areas. These deserts are characterized by extreme heat, limited vegetation, and scarce water resources. Despite the harsh conditions, the deserts are home to a variety of wildlife, including gazelles, antelopes, and lions. The coastal ecosystems, including coral reefs and mangrove forests, are also important habitats for marine life, such as turtles, dolphins, and whales.

Natural Highlights

  • Lag Badana National Park: a protected area known for its diverse wildlife, including lions, leopards, and elephants.
  • Daallo Forest: a dense forest reserve that is home to a variety of bird species and primate populations.
  • Golis Mountains: a mountain range that offers hiking and trekking opportunities, with stunning views of the surrounding landscape.
  • Juba River: a major river that flows through the southern region, providing water for irrigation and drinking, and supporting a variety of aquatic life.
  • Las Geel: a series of rock art sites that feature ancient cave paintings and petroglyphs, showcasing the country's rich cultural heritage.
  • Bari Coast: a scenic coastline that is known for its beaches, coves, and coral reefs, making it a popular destination for snorkeling and diving.
