# Zhejiang Culture - Food, Traditions & Etiquette

Zhejiang, a province in eastern China, has a distinct culture shaped by its rich history, stunning natural scenery, and vibrant economic development, making it an exciting destination to explore and experience.

[SECTION] Cultural Identity [/SECTION]
Zhejiang's culture is defined by its unique blend of traditional and modern elements, with a strong emphasis on education, entrepreneurship, and innovation. The province has a long history of intellectual and artistic achievements, with notable figures such as Su Shi, a famous poet and statesman of the Song Dynasty, and Huang Zongxi, a renowned philosopher and historian. The ethnic makeup of Zhejiang is predominantly Han Chinese, with a small percentage of minority groups, including the She and Tujia ethnic groups. The official language is Mandarin, but the local dialect, known as Wu, is widely spoken and has its own distinct characteristics.

Zhejiang's cultural identity is also influenced by its strategic location, with the province bordering the East China Sea and the Yangtze River Delta. This has facilitated trade, cultural exchange, and migration, shaping the province's cuisine, art, and traditions. The province is home to many ancient towns, such as Wuzhen and Xitang, which have preserved their traditional architecture and way of life, offering a glimpse into Zhejiang's rich cultural heritage. The province's cultural landscape is also characterized by its many museums, galleries, and cultural centers, which showcase its history, art, and traditions.

The province's cultural identity is also reflected in its vibrant festivals and celebrations, which are an integral part of Zhejiang's social fabric. The Qiantang River Tide Festival, for example, is a major celebration that showcases the province's rich cultural heritage, with traditional music, dance, and theater performances. The festival is held annually in the city of Hangzhou, the capital of Zhejiang, and attracts millions of visitors from across the country.

[SECTION] Etiquette: Do's and Don'ts [/SECTION]
**Do:**
- Use both hands when giving or receiving something, as a sign of respect
- Try to learn some basic Mandarin phrases, such as "hello" (nǐ hǎo) and "thank you" (xiè xiè)
- Remove your shoes before entering a traditional home or temple
- Use chopsticks correctly, with the top chopstick between your thumb and index finger, and the bottom chopstick between your middle finger and ring finger
- Respect for elders and authority figures is deeply ingrained in Chinese culture, so be sure to show deference to those who are older or in positions of authority
- Learn about Chinese customs and traditions, such as the importance of saving face and avoiding conflict

**Don't:**
- Don't finish a meal completely, as this implies that the host did not provide enough food
- Don't leave your chopsticks standing upright in your rice bowl, as this is reminiscent of a funeral ritual
- Don't point with your chopsticks, as this is considered rude
- Don't touch or handle sacred objects, such as Buddha statues or ancestral tablets
- Don't discuss sensitive topics, such as politics or Taiwan, in public or with strangers
- Don't tip, as this is not customary in China and may even be considered impolite in some situations
