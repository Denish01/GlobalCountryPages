Location & Borders

Guangdong is situated in the southernmost part of China, with its capital city Guangzhou being a major urban center in the region. The region is bordered by the provinces of Fujian to the northeast, Jiangxi to the north, Hunan to the northwest, and Guangxi to the west. To the southeast, Guangdong borders the special administrative regions of Hong Kong and Macau, both of which are located on the Pearl River Delta. The region's coastline stretches for over 4,300 kilometers, with the South China Sea to the east and the Gulf of Tonkin to the south.

The location of Guangdong has played a significant role in its history and economy, with the region being an important center for trade and cultural exchange between China and the rest of the world. The Pearl River Delta, which includes the cities of Guangzhou, Shenzhen, and Dongguan, is one of the most densely populated and economically developed regions in China. The delta is also an important hub for international trade, with the ports of Guangzhou and Shenzhen being among the busiest in the world.

Terrain & Landscape

The terrain of Guangdong is diverse, with mountains, rivers, and coastal plains all present in the region. The Nanling Mountains, which stretch across northern Guangdong, are the highest mountain range in the region, with peaks reaching elevations of over 1,800 meters. The mountains are covered in dense forests, with a wide range of flora and fauna, including many species of trees, birds, and mammals. The rivers of Guangdong, including the Pearl River, the East River, and the North River, are all important sources of water and transportation for the region.

The coastal plains of Guangdong are generally flat and low-lying, with many areas being below sea level. The delta region, which includes the cities of Guangzhou, Shenzhen, and Dongguan, is a densely populated and economically developed area, with many industrial and commercial centers. The coastline of Guangdong is also home to many islands, including Hainan Island, which is located off the southern coast of the region. The islands are popular tourist destinations, with many beaches, resorts, and natural attractions.

The region's diverse terrain and landscape support a wide range of ecosystems, including tropical rainforests, mangrove swamps, and coral reefs. The tropical rainforests of Guangdong are home to many species of plants and animals, including the endangered Guangdong gibbons and the Asiatic black bear. The mangrove swamps, which are found along the coastline, provide important habitats for many species of fish, birds, and mammals. The coral reefs, which are located off the coast of Guangdong, are home to many species of fish, including the endangered humphead wrasse.
