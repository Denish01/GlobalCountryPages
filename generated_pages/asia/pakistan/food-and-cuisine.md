# Pakistan Food Guide — Dishes, Street Food & Dining

When visiting Pakistan, be sure to try the diverse and flavorful dishes that showcase the country's rich culinary heritage, including popular items like **Biryani**, **Nihari**, and **Kebabs**.

[FACTBOX]
Staple Foods: Basmati rice, wheat, lentils, and chickpeas
National Dish: Nihari, a slow-cooked stew made with lamb or beef
Meal Times: 7-10 am for breakfast, 12-2 pm for lunch, and 7-10 pm for dinner
Tipping at Restaurants: 10% of the total bill is customary
Dietary Notes: Halal, with many vegetarian and vegan options available
[/FACTBOX]

[SECTION] Food Culture Overview [/SECTION]
Pakistan's cuisine is a unique blend of **Mughal**, **Punjabi**, and **Middle Eastern** influences, with a focus on rich and aromatic spices, herbs, and other ingredients. The country's strategic location at the crossroads of Asia has resulted in a diverse culinary landscape, with different regions offering their own specialty dishes and cooking styles. From the spicy **Karachi**-style **Biryani** to the rich **Lahori**-style **Nihari**, Pakistan's food culture is a true reflection of its cultural and geographic diversity.

The **Punjabi** region is known for its hearty and flavorful dishes, such as **Sarson ka Saag** (spinach curry) and **Makki ki Roti** (corn bread), while the **Sindh** region is famous for its **Biryani** and **Kebabs**. The **NWFP** (North-West Frontier Province) region, now known as **Khyber Pakhtunkhwa**, is renowned for its **Kebabs** and **Chapli Kebab**, a specialty of **Peshawar**. The **Balochistan** region is known for its **Sajji**, a slow-cooked lamb dish, and **Khaddi Kebab**, a type of **Kebab** cooked in a pit.

Pakistan's cuisine has also been influenced by its **Islamic** heritage, with a focus on **halal** ingredients and cooking methods. The country's **street food** scene is also vibrant, with popular items like **Samosas**, **Pakora**, and **Kebabs** available at **food stalls** and **markets** throughout the country.

[SECTION] Dining Customs [/SECTION]
- **Eating with the right hand**: it is customary to eat with the right hand, as the left hand is considered unclean
- **Respecting elders**: it is customary to respect elders and guests by offering them the best food and seats
- **Trying a little of everything**: it is customary to try a little of everything on the table, as a sign of respect for the host
- **Not finishing a meal completely**: it is customary to leave a little food on the plate, as a sign that the host has provided enough food
- **Not eating in public during Ramadan**: it is customary to refrain from eating in public during the month of Ramadan, as a sign of respect for those who are fasting
- **Dressing modestly**: it is customary to dress modestly when dining in public, especially in rural areas or during religious holidays
