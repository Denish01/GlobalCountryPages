# Doing Business in Bhutan — Investment, FDI & Opportunities

Doing business in Bhutan can be challenging due to its unique approach to economic development, which prioritizes **Gross National Happiness** over traditional measures of economic growth, but the country offers opportunities for investment in key sectors such as tourism, hydroelectric power, and agriculture.

[SECTION] Business Environment [/SECTION]
Bhutan's business environment is characterized by a unique blend of traditional and modern elements. The government has implemented various policies to promote foreign investment, including the establishment of the **Bhutan Investment Authority** and the introduction of a **Foreign Direct Investment Policy**. However, the country's bureaucracy can be slow and cumbersome, and corruption is a significant concern. The government has taken steps to address these issues, including the introduction of an **Anti-Corruption Act** and the establishment of an **Anti-Corruption Commission**. Despite these challenges, Bhutan offers a unique and unspoiled environment that can be attractive to investors looking for new opportunities.

The government's attitude towards foreign investment is generally positive, and the country has taken steps to simplify the investment process. However, the government's priority on **Gross National Happiness** means that economic development is balanced with social and environmental considerations. This approach can make it challenging for investors to navigate the regulatory environment, but it also offers opportunities for innovative and sustainable investments.

Bhutan's economy is heavily dependent on **hydroelectric power** and **tourism**, and the government is seeking to diversify the economy through investments in other sectors such as **agriculture** and **manufacturing**. The country has a highly literate and skilled workforce, and the government offers various incentives to attract foreign investment.

[SECTION] Foreign Direct Investment [/SECTION]
- FDI trends: Bhutan has seen a significant increase in FDI inflows in recent years, particularly in the **tourism** and **hydroelectric power** sectors.
- Main investing countries: India, Japan, and the United States are among the top investing countries in Bhutan.
- Incentives: The government offers various incentives to attract FDI, including tax breaks, subsidies, and investment promotion schemes.
- Restricted sectors: The government has restricted FDI in certain sectors, including **defense**, **tobacco**, and **alcohol** production.

[SECTION] Legal & Regulatory Framework [/SECTION]
- Business laws: Bhutan has a comprehensive set of business laws, including the **Companies Act** and the **Foreign Direct Investment Policy**.
- Intellectual property protection: Bhutan has signed several international agreements on intellectual property protection, including the **Berne Convention** and the **Paris Convention**.
- Labor laws: Bhutan has a set of labor laws that protect workers' rights, including the **Labor Act** and the **Employment Act**.
- Dispute resolution: Bhutan has a well-established dispute resolution mechanism, including the **Bhutan National Legal Institute** and the **Royal Court of Justice**.
- Tax laws: Bhutan has a comprehensive set of tax laws, including the **Income Tax Act** and the **Sales Tax Act**.

[SECTION] Challenges & Risks [/SECTION]
- Business risks: Bhutan's economy is heavily dependent on **hydroelectric power** and **tourism**, making it vulnerable to external shocks.
- Political risk: Bhutan's unique approach to economic development and its reliance on **Gross National Happiness** can make it challenging for investors to navigate the regulatory environment.
- Market limitations: Bhutan's small market size and limited access to raw materials and markets can make it challenging for investors to achieve economies of scale.
- Currency risks: Bhutan's currency, the **Ngultrum**, is pegged to the Indian Rupee, making it vulnerable to fluctuations in the Indian economy.
- Infrastructure risks: Bhutan's infrastructure is still developing, and investors may face challenges in accessing basic services such as electricity, water, and transportation.

[SECTION] Free Zones & Incentives [/SECTION]
- Special economic zones: Bhutan has established two special economic zones, the **Thimphu TechPark** and the **Pasakha Industrial Estate**, to attract foreign investment.
- Tax incentives: The government offers tax breaks and subsidies to investors in certain sectors, including **tourism** and **hydroelectric power**.
- Investment promotion agencies: The **Bhutan Investment Authority** and the **Bhutan National Legal Institute** provide support and guidance to investors.
- Other incentives: The government offers other incentives, including **training and capacity building** programs and **access to finance** schemes.
