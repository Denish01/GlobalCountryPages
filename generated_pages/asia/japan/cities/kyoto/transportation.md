# Getting Around Kyoto — Transport & Travel Guide

To get around in Kyoto, you can utilize a variety of transportation options, including an extensive rail network, buses, and taxis, as well as walk or bike through the city's historic neighborhoods.

[SECTION] Getting There [/SECTION]
Kyoto is easily accessible by air, with two main international airports serving the city: Kansai International Airport (KIX) and Osaka International Airport (ITM). Kansai International Airport is a major hub with direct flights to many international destinations, including North America, Europe, and Asia. Osaka International Airport, also known as Itami Airport, is a smaller airport that primarily handles domestic flights, but it also receives some international flights from neighboring countries. Overland entry points into Kyoto include bus and train stations, with frequent services connecting the city to other major Japanese cities, such as Tokyo and Osaka.

For travelers coming from other parts of Japan, the **bullet train**, also known as the Shinkansen, is a convenient option, with frequent services connecting Kyoto to other major cities. The city is also accessible by bus, with many companies operating services from other parts of Japan. Additionally, Kyoto has a well-developed network of highways and roads, making it possible to drive to the city from other parts of Japan. However, driving in Kyoto can be challenging due to the city's narrow streets and limited parking options.

[SECTION] Domestic Flights [/SECTION]
Domestic flights within Japan are a convenient way to travel long distances, and Kyoto is connected to many other cities by air. Japan Airlines, All Nippon Airways, and Japan Air System are some of the main domestic airlines operating in Japan. The main routes from Kyoto include flights to Tokyo, Osaka, and other major cities. Booking domestic flights in advance is recommended, especially during peak travel seasons. Approximate costs for domestic flights vary depending on the route and airline, but expect to pay around $100-$300 for a one-way ticket.

[SECTION] Trains & Rail [/SECTION]
Kyoto has an extensive and efficient rail network, with many train lines connecting the city to other parts of Japan. The **Shinkansen** is a high-speed train that connects Kyoto to other major cities, including Tokyo and Osaka. Other train lines, such as the JR Kyoto Line and the Kintetsu Kyoto Line, provide access to surrounding cities and neighborhoods. The train network in Kyoto is well-developed, with many stations and frequent services. Booking train tickets in advance is recommended, especially for popular routes, and can be done online or at train stations.

[SECTION] Buses & Coaches [/SECTION]
Intercity buses are a convenient and affordable way to travel to and from Kyoto, with many companies operating services from the city. The main bus terminal in Kyoto is the Kyoto Station Bus Terminal, which offers connections to many other cities in Japan. Bus companies such as JR Bus and Willer Bus operate frequent services from Kyoto to other parts of Japan. The quality of buses in Japan is generally high, with comfortable seats and clean vehicles. Costs for bus travel vary depending on the route and company, but expect to pay around $20-$50 for a one-way ticket.

[SECTION] Driving [/SECTION]
- To drive in Kyoto, you will need to obtain a Japanese driver's license or an International Driving Permit.
- Road conditions in Kyoto are generally good, with well-maintained highways and roads.
- Fuel costs in Japan are relatively high, with gasoline prices around $1.50 per liter.
- Car rental companies such as Toyota Rent-a-Car and Nissan Rent-a-Car have offices in Kyoto, offering a range of vehicles for rent.
- Hazards to watch out for when driving in Kyoto include narrow streets, pedestrians, and cyclists.
- Parking in Kyoto can be challenging, with limited spaces available, especially in the city center.

[SECTION] Transport Tips [/SECTION]
- When using public transportation in Kyoto, it's a good idea to purchase a **prepaid IC card**, such as an ICOCA or SUICA card, which can be used to ride trains, buses, and subways.
- Be aware of **peak travel seasons**, such as cherry blossom season and Golden Week, when transportation can be crowded and booked up.
- Download the **Hyperdia** app, which provides route information and schedules for trains, buses, and subways in Japan.
- Be mindful of **train etiquette**, such as giving up your seat to elderly or disabled passengers.
- When taking a taxi, make sure to check the **meter** to ensure you are not being overcharged.
- Consider purchasing a **Kyoto City Bus One-day Pass**, which provides unlimited travel on city buses for a day.
- Be aware of **scams** targeting tourists, such as overpriced taxi rides or fake train tickets.
- Learn some basic **Japanese phrases**, such as "konnichiwa" (hello) and "arigatou" (thank you), to help you navigate transportation in Kyoto.
