# Doing Business in Turkmenistan — Investment, FDI & Opportunities

Doing business in Turkmenistan can be challenging due to its complex regulatory environment and limited economic transparency, but the country offers significant investment opportunities in key sectors such as energy, agriculture, and manufacturing.

[SECTION] Business Environment [/SECTION]
Turkmenistan's business environment is characterized by a complex regulatory framework and a high level of bureaucracy, which can make it difficult for foreign investors to navigate. The government has implemented various measures to attract foreign investment, including the creation of special economic zones and the introduction of tax incentives. However, the country's **state-dominated economy** and limited economic transparency can create challenges for investors. Corruption is also a significant issue in Turkmenistan, and companies may encounter **bribery** and other forms of corruption when dealing with government officials.

The government's attitude towards foreign investment is generally positive, and the country has signed several agreements to promote foreign investment, including the **Foreign Investment Law**. However, the implementation of these agreements can be slow, and investors may face significant delays and bureaucratic hurdles when trying to establish a business in the country. Despite these challenges, Turkmenistan offers significant opportunities for investment, particularly in the energy and agriculture sectors.

Turkmenistan's economy is heavily reliant on **natural gas exports**, and the country has significant reserves of oil and gas. The government has implemented various measures to diversify the economy, including the development of the manufacturing and agriculture sectors. However, the country's **lack of economic transparency** and **limited access to credit** can create challenges for investors.

[SECTION] Legal & Regulatory Framework [/SECTION]
- The **Civil Code** provides the framework for business and commercial activities
- The **Foreign Investment Law** provides protection for foreign investors and outlines the procedures for establishing a business
- The **Tax Code** outlines the tax regime for businesses, including corporate tax rates and exemptions
- The **Labor Code** provides protection for workers and outlines the procedures for hiring and firing employees
- The **Arbitration Law** provides a framework for resolving disputes between businesses
- The **Intellectual Property Law** provides protection for intellectual property rights, including patents and trademarks

[SECTION] Challenges & Risks [/SECTION]
- **Corruption** is a significant risk for businesses, and companies may encounter bribery and other forms of corruption
- The **regulatory environment** can be complex and bureaucratic, making it difficult for businesses to navigate
- The **currency** can be volatile, and exchange rates can fluctuate significantly
- The **energy sector** is dominated by the state, and private companies may face significant competition from state-owned enterprises
- The **infrastructure** can be underdeveloped, particularly outside of the capital city
- The **political risk** is significant, and the government has a history of instability and authoritarianism

[SECTION] Free Zones & Incentives [/SECTION]
- The government has established a number of **free economic zones** to attract foreign investment, including the Turkmenbashi Free Economic Zone and the Mary Free Economic Zone
- The government offers various **tax incentives**, including exemptions from corporate tax and value-added tax
- The government provides **subsidies** for businesses, including support for the development of infrastructure and the purchase of equipment
- The government has established an **investment promotion agency** to attract foreign investment and provide support for investors
- The government offers **visas** and **work permits** for foreign employees, making it easier for companies to hire international staff
