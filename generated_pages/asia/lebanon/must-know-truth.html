Historical Context

Lebanon has a rich and complex history, with various civilizations and empires vying for control over the region. The Phoenicians, a Semitic people, established a thriving civilization in the area from 1500 to 300 BCE. The Phoenicians were known for their advanced maritime trade networks and their significant contributions to the development of the alphabet. Following the Phoenicians, Lebanon was conquered by the Assyrians, Persians, and Romans, each leaving their mark on the region's culture and architecture. In 635 CE, Lebanon was conquered by the Arabs, who introduced Islam to the region.

The Ottoman Empire ruled Lebanon from 1516 to 1918, during which time the region experienced significant economic and cultural growth. Following World War I, Lebanon was placed under French mandate by the League of Nations, which lasted until 1943. On November 22, 1943, Lebanon gained its independence from France, with the establishment of the Lebanese Republic. Since then, Lebanon has experienced periods of stability and instability, including a civil war from 1975 to 1990.

Lebanon's strategic location at the crossroads of Europe, Asia, and Africa has made it an important center for trade and cultural exchange throughout history. The country's diverse landscape, which includes mountains, valleys, and a coastline along the Mediterranean Sea, has also played a significant role in shaping its history and culture. Today, Lebanon is a sovereign state with a parliamentary democracy system of government.

The country's history has been marked by significant events, including the Israeli occupation of southern Lebanon from 1982 to 2000, and the Syrian occupation from 1976 to 2005. In 2005, former Prime Minister Rafik Hariri was assassinated, leading to widespread protests and the withdrawal of Syrian troops from Lebanon. Since then, Lebanon has continued to face challenges, including a refugee crisis and ongoing political instability.

Challenges & Context

  • The country faces significant environmental challenges, including deforestation, soil erosion, and water pollution, which threaten its natural resources and biodiversity.
  • Lebanon has a large public debt, which stands at over 150% of GDP, making it one of the most indebted countries in the world.
  • The country is home to over 1 million Syrian refugees, who have fled the conflict in Syria, placing a significant burden on Lebanon's infrastructure and resources.
  • Lebanon's electricity sector is in crisis, with frequent power outages and a significant reliance on imported fuel, which is a major strain on the country's economy.
  • The country's transportation infrastructure is in need of upgrade, with congested roads and a lack of public transportation options, which hinders economic growth and development.

What Gets Overlooked

  • Lebanon has a thriving arts scene, with numerous galleries, museums, and festivals, which showcase the country's rich cultural heritage and creativity.
  • The country is home to several ancient forests, including the Cedars of God, which are some of the oldest and most resilient trees in the world.
  • Lebanon has a growing startup ecosystem, with numerous incubators, accelerators, and venture capital firms, which support innovation and entrepreneurship.
  • The country has a strong tradition of hospitality, with visitors often commenting on the warm welcome they receive from the Lebanese people.
  • Lebanon is home to several UNESCO World Heritage Sites, including the ancient city of Tyre and the Qadisha Valley, which recognize the country's rich cultural and natural heritage.
