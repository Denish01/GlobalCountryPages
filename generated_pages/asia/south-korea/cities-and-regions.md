[SECTION] How South Korea Is Divided [/SECTION]
South Korea is divided into 17 administrative divisions, which include six metropolitan cities (Seoul, Busan, Daegu, Incheon, Gwangju, and Ulsan), one special city (Sejong), and 10 provinces (Gyeonggi, Gangwon, North Chungcheong, South Chungcheong, North Jeolla, South Jeolla, North Gyeongsang, South Gyeongsang, and Jeju). This structure allows for efficient governance and administration of the country's various regions. The metropolitan cities have a higher degree of autonomy, while the provinces are further divided into counties and cities.

The administrative divisions in South Korea are designed to promote regional development and balance. For example, the southeastern region, which includes Busan and Ulsan, is a major industrial hub, while the southwestern region, which includes Gwangju and Jeolla, is known for its agricultural production. The government has implemented policies to support the development of less populated regions, such as the northeastern province of Gangwon, which is a popular tourist destination.

[SECTION] Major Cities [/SECTION]
**Seoul** (10.2M)
Role: capital, economic hub, cultural center
Known For: Seoul is a bustling metropolis with a rich history, vibrant culture, and world-class amenities. The city is home to many historic landmarks, including the Gyeongbokgung Palace and the Bukchon Hanok Village.
Best For: history buffs, foodies, shopaholics

**Busan** (3.4M)
Role: port city, economic hub
Known For: Busan is a bustling port city with a rich maritime history and a thriving seafood industry. The city is also known for its beautiful beaches, such as Haeundae and Gwangalli.
Best For: beachgoers, seafood lovers, shoppers

**Incheon** (2.9M)
Role: port city, economic hub
Known For: Incheon is a major port city located near Seoul, with a rich history and a thriving economy. The city is also home to the Incheon International Airport, which is one of the busiest airports in Asia.
Best For: business travelers, shoppers, foodies

**Daegu** (2.5M)
Role: economic hub, cultural center
Known For: Daegu is a major city in southeastern Korea, known for its rich cultural heritage and vibrant arts scene. The city is home to many historic landmarks, including the Daegu Cathedral and the Gyeongsanggamyeong Park.
Best For: culture lovers, history buffs, shoppers

**Gwangju** (1.5M)
Role: cultural center, economic hub
Known For: Gwangju is a city in southwestern Korea, known for its rich cultural heritage and vibrant arts scene. The city is home to many historic landmarks, including the Gwangju National Museum and the May 18th National Cemetery.
Best For: culture lovers, history buffs, foodies

**Daejeon** (1.5M)
Role: economic hub, educational center
Known For: Daejeon is a major city in central Korea, known for its thriving economy and rich educational resources. The city is home to many universities and research institutions, including the Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology.
Best For: students, researchers, business travelers

**Ulsan** (1.2M)
Role: industrial hub, port city
Known For: Ulsan is a major industrial city in southeastern Korea, known for its thriving automotive and shipbuilding industries. The city is also home to many historic landmarks, including the Ulsan Museum of Art and the Taehwa River Park.
Best For: industrial tourists, business travelers, nature lovers

[SECTION] Regional Differences [/SECTION]
- The northern regions, such as Gangwon and North Chungcheong, are generally cooler and more mountainous, with a stronger emphasis on agriculture and outdoor activities.
- The southern regions, such as South Jeolla and South Gyeongsang, are generally warmer and more coastal, with a stronger emphasis on seafood and tourism.
- The eastern regions, such as North Gyeongsang and South Gyeongsang, are generally more industrial and urban, with a stronger emphasis on manufacturing and trade.
- The western regions, such as Gyeonggi and North Jeolla, are generally more urban and developed, with a stronger emphasis on technology and education.

[SECTION] Getting Around [/SECTION]
South Korea has a well-developed transportation network, with many options for domestic travel. The country has a comprehensive high-speed rail network, with trains connecting major cities like Seoul, Busan, and Daejeon. Domestic flights are also available, with many airports located throughout the country. Bus travel is another popular option, with many companies offering routes between cities and towns. Driving is also possible, with a well-maintained highway system and many rental car options available. However, driving in Korea can be challenging, especially for foreigners, due to the country's unique traffic culture and language barriers.

For travelers, the most convenient option is often the train, which is fast, efficient, and relatively affordable. The Korea Train eXpress (KTX) is a high-speed train that connects major cities, while the Saemaeul and Mugunghwa trains offer more affordable options for shorter distances. Buses are also a good option, with many companies offering comfortable and convenient services. For longer distances, domestic flights may be the best option, with many airlines offering frequent flights between major cities. Overall, South Korea's transportation network makes it easy to get around the country, with many options available to suit different budgets and preferences.
