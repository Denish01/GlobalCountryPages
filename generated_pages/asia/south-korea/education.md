# South Korea Education — Schools, Universities & Student Guide

The education system in South Korea is highly competitive and rigorous, with a strong emphasis on science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM) fields, as well as a broad range of academic subjects, making it an attractive destination for both domestic and international students.

[SECTION] Education System Overview [/SECTION]
The education system in South Korea is divided into three main stages: primary, secondary, and tertiary education. Primary education, which is compulsory, lasts for six years and focuses on basic subjects such as Korean, mathematics, and science. Secondary education is also compulsory and lasts for three years, with students attending either an academic or vocational high school. Tertiary education, which includes universities and colleges, is highly competitive and offers a wide range of academic programs. The quality of education in South Korea is generally high, with a strong emphasis on **standardized testing** and academic achievement. Public schools are free, but private schools, known as hagwons, are also popular and offer additional instruction and support.

The education system in South Korea is highly competitive, with a strong emphasis on **academic achievement** and **standardized testing**. Students typically attend school from 8 am to 4 pm, with additional hours spent on extracurricular activities and private tutoring. The system is also highly centralized, with the government playing a significant role in setting curriculum and standards. Despite the challenges, the education system in South Korea has produced some of the most highly educated and skilled workers in the world.

The public and private sectors play different roles in the education system in South Korea. Public schools are free and provide a basic education, while private schools offer additional instruction and support. Private schools, known as hagwons, are highly popular and offer a range of programs, from **English language instruction** to **mathematics and science**. The government has implemented various reforms to improve the education system, including increasing funding for public schools and expanding access to higher education.

[SECTION] International Schools [/SECTION]
- International schools are widely available in major cities, offering a range of curricula, including **International Baccalaureate (IB)**, **British**, and **American** programs.
- The cost of attending an international school can be high, with tuition fees ranging from $10,000 to $20,000 per year.
- Many international schools offer additional programs and services, such as **English language support** and **extracurricular activities**.
- International schools are located in major cities, including Seoul, Busan, and Daejeon.
- Some international schools also offer **boarding facilities** for students who need to live away from home.

[SECTION] Studying as a Foreigner [/SECTION]
- To study in South Korea, international students must obtain a **student visa**, which requires proof of admission to a university or college, as well as proof of financial support.
- Language requirements vary depending on the program and institution, but most universities require **English language proficiency**, with some also offering **Korean language courses**.
- Scholarships are available for international students, including government-funded scholarships and university-funded scholarships.
- Living costs for students in South Korea can be relatively high, with **rent**, **food**, and **transportation** costs averaging around $1,000 to $1,500 per month.
- International students can also work part-time to support themselves, with many universities offering **part-time job opportunities** on campus.
- The government has implemented various initiatives to support international students, including **language support programs** and **cultural orientation**.

[SECTION] Education Quality & Challenges [/SECTION]
- The education system in South Korea is highly competitive, with a strong emphasis on **academic achievement** and **standardized testing**.
- One of the major challenges facing the education system is **overemphasis on rote memorization**, which can limit critical thinking and creativity.
- The system is also highly centralized, which can limit flexibility and innovation in the classroom.
- Recent reforms have aimed to address these challenges, including the introduction of **competency-based education** and **project-based learning**.
- The government has also increased funding for education, with a focus on improving **teacher training** and **school infrastructure**.
