Business Environment

The overall business climate in Jordan is generally favorable, with a relatively stable economy and a strategic location at the crossroads of the Middle East, Europe, and Africa. The government has implemented various reforms to improve the business environment, including simplifying procedures for starting a business and reducing bureaucracy. However, corruption remains a significant challenge, and businesses may still face difficulties in dealing with government agencies and obtaining necessary permits. The government's attitude toward foreign investment is generally positive, with various incentives and investment promotion agencies in place to attract foreign investors.

The level of bureaucracy in Jordan can be relatively high, with multiple agencies involved in the business registration process and other regulatory procedures. However, the government has made efforts to streamline these processes and reduce the time and cost required to start a business. Despite these challenges, Jordan has a relatively well-developed infrastructure, with a modern transportation network and a range of amenities and services available to businesses.

The government has also implemented various initiatives to promote entrepreneurship and innovation, including the establishment of business incubators and accelerators, as well as funding programs for start-ups and small businesses. These initiatives have helped to foster a vibrant entrepreneurial ecosystem in Jordan, with a growing number of start-ups and small businesses operating in the country.

Foreign Direct Investment

  • FDI inflows to Jordan have been increasing in recent years, with the country attracting a range of foreign investors from countries such as the United Arab Emirates, Saudi Arabia, and the United States.
  • The main investing countries in Jordan are the United Arab Emirates, Saudi Arabia, and the United States, with investments focused on sectors such as information technology, manufacturing, and tourism.
  • The government offers a range of incentives to attract foreign investors, including tax breaks, subsidies, and investment promotion programs.
  • Restricted sectors for foreign investment include defense, security, and certain areas of the media and telecommunications sectors.
  • The Jordanian government has implemented various initiatives to promote foreign investment, including the establishment of the Jordanian Investment Commission, which provides support and guidance to foreign investors.

Legal & Regulatory Framework

  • The business laws in Jordan are relatively well-developed, with a range of laws and regulations in place to govern business activities, including the Companies Law, the Commercial Law, and the Labor Law.
  • Intellectual property protection is relatively strong in Jordan, with laws and regulations in place to protect patents, trademarks, and copyrights.
  • Labor laws in Jordan are relatively strict, with a range of protections in place for workers, including minimum wage laws, working hour regulations, and health and safety standards.
  • Dispute resolution mechanisms are available in Jordan, including arbitration and mediation services.
  • The government has implemented various initiatives to improve the business environment, including the establishment of a one-stop shop for business registration and the introduction of online services for business licensing and permits.
  • The legal and regulatory framework in Jordan is relatively stable, with a range of laws and regulations in place to govern business activities and protect the rights of investors.

Challenges & Risks

  • The business environment in Jordan can be challenging, with a range of risks and obstacles that need to be carefully considered, including corruption, bureaucracy, and limited access to finance.
  • Political risk is a significant concern in Jordan, with the country located in a region that is prone to instability and conflict.
  • Market limitations are a challenge in Jordan, with a relatively small domestic market and limited access to regional markets.
  • Currency risks are a concern in Jordan, with the Jordanian dinar pegged to the US dollar and subject to fluctuations in the value of the dollar.
  • Security risks are a concern in Jordan, with the country located in a region that is prone to terrorism and other security threats.
  • Infrastructure challenges are a concern in Jordan, with a range of issues related to transportation, energy, and water supply that need to be addressed.

Free Zones & Incentives

  • Jordan has a range of special economic zones, including the Aqaba Special Economic Zone, the Amman Development Corridor, and the Ma'an Development Area, which offer a range of incentives and benefits to investors, including tax breaks, subsidies, and streamlined regulatory procedures.
  • The government offers a range of tax incentives to investors, including exemptions from income tax, sales tax, and customs duties.
  • Investment promotion agencies, such as the Jordanian Investment Commission, provide support and guidance to investors, including assistance with business registration, licensing, and permits.
  • The government has implemented various initiatives to promote investment in certain sectors, such as information technology and renewable energy, including the establishment of specialized investment zones and the provision of funding and other support to investors.
