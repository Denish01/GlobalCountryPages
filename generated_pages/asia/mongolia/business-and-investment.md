# Doing Business in Mongolia — Investment, FDI & Opportunities

Doing business in Mongolia can be challenging due to its bureaucratic and corrupt environment, but the country offers significant investment opportunities in key sectors such as mining, agriculture, and infrastructure development.

[FACTBOX]
Ease of Doing Business: 74th (out of 190 countries, World Bank ranking 2020)
Corporate Tax Rate: 20%
FDI Inflow: $2.3 billion (annual average, 2015-2020)
Special Economic Zones: Yes, 4
Key Industries: Mining, Agriculture, Manufacturing
Currency Stability: Moderate
[/FACTBOX]

[SECTION] Business Environment [/SECTION]
Mongolia's business environment is characterized by a mix of opportunities and challenges. The government has been actively promoting foreign investment, particularly in the mining and infrastructure sectors, and has introduced various incentives to attract investors. However, the country still struggles with bureaucratic red tape, corruption, and a lack of transparency in business dealings. The government has made efforts to improve the business climate, including the establishment of a one-stop shop for business registration and the introduction of online portals for tax payments and other government services.

The attitude of the government towards foreign investment is generally positive, with a focus on attracting foreign capital to support economic growth and development. However, the government has also been criticized for its lack of transparency and consistency in its policies and regulations, which can create uncertainty and risks for investors. The level of bureaucracy in Mongolia is relatively high, with multiple agencies and ministries involved in the business registration and licensing process. Corruption is also a significant issue, with many businesses reporting that they have to pay bribes to obtain licenses, permits, and other government services.

The government has introduced various measures to reduce corruption and improve transparency, including the establishment of an anti-corruption agency and the introduction of new laws and regulations to prevent corrupt practices. However, more needs to be done to address these issues and create a more favorable business environment. Despite these challenges, Mongolia offers significant opportunities for investors, particularly in the mining and infrastructure sectors, where the country has significant natural resources and a growing demand for goods and services.

[SECTION] Foreign Direct Investment [/SECTION]
- Mongolia has attracted significant foreign direct investment (FDI) in recent years, particularly in the mining sector, with main investing countries including China, Canada, and Australia.
- The government has introduced various incentives to attract FDI, including tax breaks, subsidies, and investment guarantees.
- The main sectors for FDI are mining, infrastructure, and manufacturing, with restricted sectors including defense, banking, and telecommunications.
- The government has also established a number of free trade agreements with neighboring countries, including China and Russia, to promote trade and investment.

[SECTION] Legal & Regulatory Framework [/SECTION]
- The business laws in Mongolia are based on the Civil Code and the Commercial Code, which provide a framework for business operations and transactions.
- Intellectual property protection is provided through the Intellectual Property Law, which covers patents, trademarks, and copyrights.
- Labor laws are governed by the Labor Law, which sets out the rights and obligations of employers and employees.
- Dispute resolution is provided through the courts and arbitration tribunals, with the option for international arbitration.
- The government has also established a number of regulatory agencies, including the Financial Regulatory Commission and the Energy Regulatory Commission, to oversee and regulate various sectors.

[SECTION] Challenges & Risks [/SECTION]
- One of the main challenges facing investors in Mongolia is the risk of **political instability**, which can affect the business environment and investment climate.
- The country is also vulnerable to **economic shocks**, particularly in the mining sector, which can have a significant impact on the economy.
- **Corruption** is a significant issue in Mongolia, with many businesses reporting that they have to pay bribes to obtain licenses, permits, and other government services.
- The country also faces significant **infrastructure challenges**, including a lack of roads, bridges, and transportation links, which can make it difficult to transport goods and services.
- **Currency risks** are also a concern, with the Mongolian currency, the tugrik, subject to significant fluctuations against major currencies.

[SECTION] Free Zones & Incentives [/SECTION]
- Mongolia has established a number of **special economic zones** (SEZs), including the Ulaanbaatar SEZ and the Darkhan SEZ, which offer tax breaks, subsidies, and other incentives to investors.
- The government has also introduced a number of **tax incentives**, including a reduced corporate tax rate of 10% for companies operating in the mining sector.
- The **Mongolian Investment Promotion Agency** (MIPA) has been established to promote investment and provide support to investors, including information on investment opportunities and procedures.
- The government has also introduced a number of **investment guarantees**, including a guarantee against expropriation and nationalization, to provide security and protection to investors.
