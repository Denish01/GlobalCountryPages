Business Environment

Brunei has a favorable business environment, with a relatively low corruption perception index ranking of 35 out of 180 countries. The government has implemented various initiatives to attract foreign investment, including the establishment of the Brunei Economic Development Board (BEDB) and the Brunei Investment Agency (BIA). However, the country's bureaucracy can be complex, and the process of obtaining necessary permits and licenses can be time-consuming. The government has also implemented various measures to reduce red tape and improve the ease of doing business, including the introduction of online registration systems and streamlined regulatory procedures.

The government's attitude towards foreign investment is generally positive, with a range of incentives and support available for investors, including tax breaks and investment allowances. However, the country's small size and limited market may pose challenges for some investors, and the government has implemented measures to encourage diversification of the economy and reduce dependence on the oil and gas sector.

Brunei has a relatively high standard of living and a well-educated workforce, with a literacy rate of over 95%. The country also has a stable political system, with a long-serving monarch and a relatively low risk of political instability.

Foreign Direct Investment

  • Brunei has a relatively low level of foreign direct investment (FDI), with a total FDI inflow of $0.2 billion in 2020.
  • The main investing countries in Brunei are Singapore, Malaysia, and the United Kingdom.
  • The government offers a range of incentives to attract FDI, including tax breaks, investment allowances, and streamlined regulatory procedures.
  • Restricted sectors for FDI include defense, energy, and telecommunications, which are subject to government regulation and licensing requirements.

Legal & Regulatory Framework

  • Brunei has a well-developed legal system, with a range of laws and regulations governing business and investment.
  • The Companies Act provides the framework for company registration and operation, while the Income Tax Act governs taxation.
  • Brunei has a range of laws and regulations protecting intellectual property, including patents, trademarks, and copyrights.
  • The Employment Act governs labor laws and regulations, including employment contracts, wages, and working conditions.
  • The Arbitration Act provides a framework for dispute resolution, including arbitration and mediation.

Challenges & Risks

  • Brunei's small size and limited market may pose challenges for some investors, particularly those seeking to export goods and services.
  • The country's dependence on oil and gas may make it vulnerable to fluctuations in global energy prices.
  • Brunei has a relatively high cost of living, which may affect the competitiveness of businesses operating in the country.
  • The country's limited infrastructure may pose challenges for investors, particularly those seeking to establish large-scale operations.
  • Brunei has a relatively low level of transparency in government decision-making and procurement processes, which may affect the ease of doing business.

Free Zones & Incentives

  • Brunei has two special economic zones, the Sungai Liang Industrial Park and the Pulau Muara Besar, which offer a range of incentives and support for investors, including tax breaks, investment allowances, and streamlined regulatory procedures.
  • The government offers a range of tax incentives, including exemptions from income tax and goods and services tax, for investors in certain sectors, such as manufacturing and tourism.
  • The Brunei Investment Agency provides support and assistance to investors, including help with registration and licensing, and access to financing and other resources.
