# Moving to Brunei — Visas, Residency & Expat Guide

To move to Brunei, you should start by researching the country's visa and residency options, understanding the cost of living, and preparing for cultural differences, and then plan your relocation step by step, considering factors such as housing, employment, and healthcare.

[SECTION] Why People Move to Brunei [/SECTION]
Brunei is a small, oil-rich country on the island of Borneo, offering a unique blend of traditional Malay culture and modern amenities, attracting expats with its **high standard of living**, **low crime rate**, and **tax-free** environment. Many expats are drawn to Brunei's **expat-friendly** community, with a significant number of foreigners working in the oil and gas industry, as well as in education and healthcare. The country's **tropical climate** and **natural beauty**, with rainforests, beaches, and coral reefs, also make it an attractive destination for nature lovers and those seeking a relaxed lifestyle.

The majority of expats in Brunei are from **Malaysia**, **Singapore**, and other **Southeast Asian** countries, although there are also significant numbers of expats from **Europe**, **Australia**, and **North America**. Brunei's **expat demographics** are diverse, with a range of ages, professions, and interests represented. In terms of **quality of life**, Brunei offers a high level of comfort and convenience, with modern amenities, good healthcare, and a range of recreational activities available.

Despite its many attractions, Brunei is a **conservative** country, with strict laws and regulations, particularly regarding **Islamic customs** and **dress codes**. Expats should be prepared to respect local traditions and customs, and to adapt to a more **laid-back** pace of life. Overall, Brunei offers a unique and rewarding expat experience, with a blend of traditional culture, modern amenities, and natural beauty.

[SECTION] Relocation Checklist [/SECTION]
1. Research Brunei's visa and residency options and choose the most suitable one for your situation.
2. Gather all necessary documents, including your passport, degree, and medical certificate.
3. Apply for a visa or work permit, if required, and wait for approval.
4. Book a flight to Brunei and arrange for accommodation, either temporarily or long-term.
5. Register with the local authorities and obtain a **residence permit**.
6. Open a **bank account** and transfer funds to Brunei.
7. Find a job or start a business, if you haven't already done so.
8. Enroll in a **health insurance** plan, as Brunei's public healthcare system is limited.
9. Learn about Brunei's **culture** and **customs**, and try to learn some basic **Malay phrases**.
10. Join **expat groups** and attend **social events** to connect with other expats and build a social network.
11. Explore Brunei's **neighborhoods** and find a suitable place to live, considering factors such as safety, amenities, and commute time.
12. Plan for **taxes** and **finances**, including understanding Brunei's tax system and planning for retirement.

[SECTION] Expat Communities [/SECTION]
- **Bandar Seri Begawan**, the capital city, has a large expat community, with many foreigners living and working in the city.
- **Seria**, a town in the Belait district, is home to many expats working in the oil and gas industry.
- **Kuala Belait**, another town in the Belait district, has a smaller but still significant expat community.
- The **Brunei Expats** Facebook group is a popular online community for expats to connect, share information, and ask questions.
- The **Internations** website has a Brunei expat forum, where members can discuss topics such as housing, jobs, and healthcare.

[SECTION] Banking & Finances [/SECTION]
- **Opening a bank account** in Brunei can be relatively straightforward, with many banks offering services in English.
- **Money transfers** can be made through banks or money transfer services, such as Western Union or PayPal.
- **Currency considerations** include the Brunei dollar, which is pegged to the Singapore dollar, and the US dollar, which is widely accepted.
- **Credit cards** are widely accepted in Brunei, particularly in tourist areas and larger towns.
- **Taxes** in Brunei are relatively low, with no personal income tax, but expats should still understand their tax obligations and plan accordingly.

[SECTION] Challenges & Honest Advice [/SECTION]
- **Culture shock** can be a significant challenge for expats in Brunei, particularly those from Western countries, due to the country's conservative laws and customs.
- **Bureaucracy** can be frustrating, with slow processing times and complex procedures for tasks such as obtaining a residence permit or opening a bank account.
- **Language barriers** can be a challenge, particularly outside of Bandar Seri Begawan, where English may not be widely spoken.
- **Housing** can be expensive, particularly in popular areas, and expats should be prepared to compromise on factors such as location and amenities.
- **Transportation** can be limited, with few public transportation options available, and expats may need to rely on taxis or private cars.
- **Social life** can be limited, particularly for those who don't speak Malay or Arabic, and expats may need to make an effort to connect with other expats and locals.
