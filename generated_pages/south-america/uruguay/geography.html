Location & Borders

Uruguay is situated in the southeastern part of South America, and it is the second-smallest country on the continent. It is bordered by two larger countries, Argentina to the west and Brazil to the north and east. The country has a coastline along the Atlantic Ocean to the south, which provides access to the ocean and facilitates trade and commerce. The coastline is approximately 660 kilometers long, and it features several important ports, including the port of Montevideo, which is the country's capital and largest city.

The country's borders with Argentina and Brazil are defined by a combination of natural and artificial boundaries. The border with Argentina is marked by the Uruguay River, which flows into the Atlantic Ocean, while the border with Brazil is defined by a series of rivers, streams, and artificial boundaries. The country's location in the southeastern part of South America makes it a strategic hub for trade and commerce between the region's larger countries.

Terrain & Landscape

Uruguay's terrain is generally flat, with some low-lying hills and coastal plains. The country's highest point is Cerro Catedral, which is approximately 514 meters above sea level. The lowest point is the Atlantic Ocean, which borders the country to the south. The country's landscape is dominated by rolling hills, grasslands, and savannas, with some forests and woodlands along the coast and in the interior. The coastal region is generally more populated and urbanized, with several important cities, including Montevideo, Punta del Este, and Colonia del Sacramento.

The country's interior is more rural and sparsely populated, with a mix of agriculture, livestock, and natural habitats. The landscape is dotted with small farms, ranches, and villages, and the countryside is generally quiet and peaceful. The country's terrain and landscape make it an attractive destination for tourists, with its rolling hills, beautiful coastline, and picturesque countryside. The country is also home to several important rivers, including the Uruguay River, which forms the border with Argentina, and the Rio de la Plata, which flows into the Atlantic Ocean.

The country's geography has played an important role in shaping its economy and culture. The country's fertile soil and mild climate make it an ideal place for agriculture and livestock, and the country is known for its high-quality beef, wool, and dairy products. The country's coastline and ports have also facilitated trade and commerce, and the country has a long history of maritime trade and navigation.
