[SECTION] How Venezuela Is Divided [/SECTION]
Venezuela's administrative structure is divided into 23 states, each with its own capital and a significant degree of autonomy. The states are further divided into municipalities, which are the basic units of local government. The country also has a capital district, which is home to the federal government and many national institutions. In addition to the states and capital district, Venezuela has a set of islands and territories that are under federal jurisdiction, including the Isla de Aves and the Isla de Patos.

The division of Venezuela into states and municipalities allows for a degree of regional autonomy and self-governance, which is reflected in the country's cultural and economic diversity. Each state has its own unique character and identity, shaped by its history, geography, and cultural traditions. From the Andean highlands to the Caribbean coast, Venezuela's regions offer a rich and varied experience for visitors and residents alike.

[SECTION] Getting Around [/SECTION]
Venezuela has a well-developed transportation network, with domestic flights, buses, and taxis available throughout the country. The road network is extensive, but driving conditions can be challenging, particularly in rural areas. The country also has a limited rail network, but it is not a major mode of transportation. For visitors, the best way to get around is often by taxi or bus, as these are widely available and relatively affordable. Domestic flights are also a good option, particularly for longer distances.

In terms of transportation, Venezuela has a number of major airports, including Simón Bolívar International Airport in Caracas and La Chinita International Airport in Maracaibo. The country is also home to a number of major bus companies, including Expresos del Oriente and Expresos Unidos, which offer routes throughout the country. For those who prefer to drive, car rental companies are available in major cities, but driving conditions can be challenging, particularly in rural areas. Overall, getting around Venezuela requires some planning and flexibility, but the country's transportation network offers a range of options for visitors and residents alike.
