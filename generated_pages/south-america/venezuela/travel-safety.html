Overall Safety Assessment

Venezuela has been experiencing a severe economic crisis, which has led to a significant increase in crime rates, particularly in major cities like Caracas and Maracaibo. Travelers should be aware of the high risk of petty theft, armed robbery, and kidnapping, as well as the presence of corrupt officials and police. Additionally, the country has been affected by social unrest, protests, and demonstrations, which can turn violent at any moment. However, with proper planning, research, and precautions, it is still possible to have a safe and enjoyable trip to Venezuela.

Despite the challenges, Venezuela is a country with a rich cultural heritage and breathtaking natural beauty, from the Andes mountains to the Caribbean coast. With a strong sense of community and hospitality, many Venezuelans are eager to share their culture and traditions with visitors. However, it is essential to be aware of the current situation and take necessary precautions to ensure a safe and successful trip. Travelers should stay informed about local conditions, avoid traveling alone at night, and be cautious when using public transportation or taxis.

Common Risks & Scams

  • Armed robbery and kidnapping are common in major cities, particularly at night
  • Petty theft and pickpocketing are prevalent in crowded areas and public transportation
  • Scams targeting tourists, such as fake police checkpoints and ATM scams, are common
  • Protests and demonstrations can turn violent, and travelers should avoid areas where they are taking place
  • Corrupt officials and police may demand bribes or try to extort money from tourists
  • Credit card skimming and fraud are common, and travelers should be cautious when using ATMs or credit cards
  • Travelers should be aware of the risk of Chikungunya and Zika virus, and take necessary precautions to prevent mosquito bites

Health & Medical

  • Vaccinations: Hepatitis A, Hepatitis B, and Typhoid fever are recommended, and Yellow Fever vaccination is required for travelers coming from or passing through certain countries
  • Water Safety: Tap water is not safe to drink, and bottled or filtered water is recommended
  • Medical Facilities: Medical facilities are limited, especially outside of major cities, and travelers may need to pay cash upfront for medical services
  • Pharmacy Access: Pharmacy access is limited, and travelers may need to bring their own medications or purchase them on the black market

Practical Safety Tips

  • Stay informed about local conditions and demonstrations through government websites and local news
  • Avoid traveling alone at night, and use reputable taxi services or ride-sharing apps
  • Keep valuables secure, and use hotel safes or lockers when available
  • Be cautious when using public transportation, and avoid carrying large amounts of cash
  • Avoid displaying signs of wealth, such as expensive jewelry or watches
  • Learn some basic Spanish phrases, and be respectful of local customs and traditions
  • Stay hydrated and bring sun protection, as the sun can be intense in Venezuela

For Solo & Women Travelers

  • Be aware of your surroundings, and avoid walking alone in isolated areas
  • Dress modestly, and avoid displaying signs of wealth or affluence
  • Be cautious when interacting with strangers, and avoid accepting rides or invitations from unknown individuals
  • Stay in touch with family and friends back home, and keep them informed of your itinerary and whereabouts
