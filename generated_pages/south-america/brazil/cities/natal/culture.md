# Natal Culture - Food, Traditions & Etiquette

Natal, a city in the northeastern part of Brazil, has a unique culture that is a blend of Portuguese, African, and indigenous influences, shaped by its history, geography, and the people who live there.

[SECTION] Cultural Identity [/SECTION]
Natal's culture is defined by its rich history, which dates back to the 16th century when the Portuguese established the city as a colonial outpost. The city's strategic location on the northeastern coast of Brazil made it an important center for trade and commerce, attracting people from various parts of the world, including Africa, Europe, and other parts of South America. As a result, Natal's culture is a unique blend of Portuguese, African, and indigenous influences, with a strong emphasis on music, dance, and folklore. The city's ethnic and linguistic makeup is predominantly Portuguese, with many residents speaking Portuguese as their first language. However, there are also significant African and indigenous influences, particularly in the city's music, dance, and art.

The city's cultural identity is also shaped by its geography, with the Atlantic Ocean and the dunes of the Genipabu Beach playing a significant role in the city's lifestyle and traditions. The city's residents, known as Natalenses, are proud of their heritage and work hard to preserve their cultural traditions, including their music, dance, and folklore. The city's cultural scene is vibrant, with many festivals and celebrations taking place throughout the year, including the famous **Festa do Divino Espírito Santo**, which is a major celebration that takes place in May.

Natal's cultural identity is also influenced by its history as a major center for the production of cotton, sugar, and other crops, which brought many immigrants to the city from other parts of Brazil and the world. Today, the city is a thriving metropolis with a diverse economy, a rich cultural scene, and a strong sense of community. The city's cultural identity is a unique blend of traditional and modern elements, with a strong emphasis on preserving its heritage and promoting its cultural traditions.

[SECTION] Etiquette: Do's and Don'ts [/SECTION]
**Do:**
- Greet people with a handshake or a kiss on the cheek, as a sign of respect and friendship
- Use formal titles such as **Senhor** or **Senhora** when addressing older people or people in positions of authority
- Try to learn a few words of Portuguese, such as **obrigado** (thank you) and **por favor** (please)
- Respect the city's cultural traditions and customs, particularly during festivals and celebrations
- Tip waiters and waitresses, as well as taxi drivers and other service providers
- Be patient and flexible, as the city's traffic and infrastructure can be challenging at times

**Don't:**
- Don't touch or handle people's personal belongings, such as their phones or wallets
- Don't take pictures of people without their permission, particularly in rural or indigenous areas
- Don't litter or throw trash on the streets, as the city takes pride in its cleanliness and beauty
- Don't use aggressive or confrontational language, as the city's residents value politeness and respect
- Don't ignore or dismiss the city's cultural traditions and customs, as they are an important part of the city's identity
- Don't drink tap water, as it may not be safe for consumption
