# Getting Around Amazonas — Transport & Travel Guide

To get around in Amazonas, you can use a combination of domestic flights, buses, taxis, and ride-hailing services, as well as drive or take a boat, depending on your destination and preferences.

[FACTBOX]
Main International Airport(s): Manaus International Airport (MAO)
Domestic Air Network: extensive
Rail Network: none
Drives On: right
Road Quality: poor
Ride-Hailing Apps: Uber, local alternatives
[/FACTBOX]

[SECTION] Getting There [/SECTION]
The main entry point to Amazonas is the Manaus International Airport (MAO), which receives direct flights from major Brazilian cities like São Paulo, Rio de Janeiro, and Brasília, as well as some international flights from neighboring countries. You can also enter Amazonas by boat, taking a ferry or a cruise along the Amazon River, which is a popular option for those who want to experience the region's stunning natural beauty. Additionally, you can take a bus or drive from neighboring states, such as Acre, Amapá, or Pará, although the road conditions can be challenging, especially during the rainy season.

If you are planning to visit Amazonas from outside Brazil, you will likely need to fly into a major Brazilian city first and then take a domestic flight to Manaus. Some airlines, such as LATAM and Gol, offer direct flights from São Paulo or Rio de Janeiro to Manaus, while others may require a layover. It's also possible to take a bus or drive from neighboring countries, such as Peru or Colombia, although this can be a long and arduous journey. Be sure to research the latest entry requirements and visa regulations before planning your trip.

[SECTION] Domestic Flights [/SECTION]
Amazonas has an extensive domestic air network, with several airlines operating flights to and from Manaus, including LATAM, Gol, and Azul. You can fly to other major cities in Brazil, such as São Paulo, Rio de Janeiro, and Brasília, as well as to smaller cities and towns in the region. Booking in advance is recommended, especially during peak travel seasons, as prices can be high and flights may sell out quickly. Approximate costs for domestic flights in Brazil can range from $100 to $500 or more, depending on the route and time of year. It's also a good idea to compare prices across different airlines and book through a reputable online travel agency or the airline's website.

[SECTION] Trains & Rail [/SECTION]
There is no rail network in Amazonas, making it necessary to rely on other modes of transportation, such as buses, taxis, and domestic flights. However, there are plans to build a railway line connecting Manaus to other major cities in Brazil, which could potentially improve transportation options in the region.

[SECTION] Buses & Coaches [/SECTION]
Several bus companies operate in Amazonas, offering intercity services to and from Manaus, as well as to smaller towns and villages in the region. Some of the main bus companies include Expresso Brasilia, Util, and Transamazonica. The quality of the buses can vary, but most are comfortable and well-maintained, with air conditioning and onboard amenities. Prices are generally affordable, with approximate costs ranging from $10 to $50 or more, depending on the route and time of year. It's a good idea to book in advance, especially during peak travel seasons, as seats may sell out quickly.

[SECTION] Driving [/SECTION]
- To drive in Amazonas, you will need a valid driver's license from your home country or an international driver's license.
- Road conditions in Amazonas can be poor, especially during the rainy season, so it's essential to exercise caution and drive slowly.
- Fuel costs can be high, especially in remote areas where gas stations may be scarce.
- Car rental companies are available in Manaus and other major cities, but it's essential to research and book in advance, especially during peak travel seasons.
- Hazards on the road can include potholes, landslides, and wildlife, so it's crucial to stay alert and drive defensively.
- It's also a good idea to consider renting a 4x4 vehicle, especially if you plan to drive on unpaved roads or in remote areas.

[SECTION] Transport Tips [/SECTION]
- Be aware of scams and overcharging, especially when taking taxis or ride-hailing services, and always agree on a price before you start your journey.
- Payment methods can vary, but credit cards and cash are widely accepted, and it's a good idea to have some local currency, the Brazilian Real, with you.
- Download ride-hailing apps, such as Uber, and other transportation apps, such as Google Maps, to help you navigate the region.
- Be mindful of your belongings, especially in crowded areas or tourist hotspots, and keep an eye on your luggage at all times.
- Learn some basic Portuguese phrases, such as "obrigado" (thank you) and "por favor" (please), to help you communicate with locals.
- Consider hiring a guide or joining a tour group, especially if you're planning to venture into remote areas or engage in outdoor activities, such as hiking or wildlife spotting.
- Stay up-to-date with the latest travel advisories and warnings, and exercise caution when traveling to areas with high crime rates or social unrest.
