[SECTION] Location & Borders [/SECTION]
Bahia is situated in the northeastern part of Brazil, bordered by nine states, including Piauí, Pernambuco, Alagoas, Sergipe, Goiás, Tocantins, Minas Gerais, and Espírito Santo. The region is surrounded by the Atlantic Ocean to the east, which provides a long coastline with numerous beaches, bays, and islands. The capital city of Bahia is Salvador, which is also the largest city in the region and a major cultural and economic center.

The location of Bahia is strategic, with the region being connected to the rest of Brazil by a network of highways, railways, and airports. The Port of Salvador is one of the busiest ports in Brazil, handling a significant amount of cargo and passenger traffic. The region's proximity to the equator and the Atlantic Ocean also makes it an important hub for international trade and commerce. The borders of Bahia are also home to several important cities, including Feira de Santana, Vitória da Conquista, and Ilhéus, which are significant urban centers in the region.

The terrain of Bahia is diverse, with a range of landscapes that include mountains, forests, rivers, and coastlines. The region is home to several mountain ranges, including the Serra da Chapada Diamantina, which is one of the highest points in northeastern Brazil. The mountains are covered with lush forests, including the Atlantic Forest, which is one of the most biodiverse ecosystems in the world. The region is also home to several important rivers, including the São Francisco River, which is one of the longest rivers in Brazil.

The coastline of Bahia is long and scenic, with numerous beaches, bays, and islands. The region is home to several important coastal cities, including Salvador, which is known for its historic center and vibrant cultural scene. The coastline is also home to several national parks and protected areas, including the Abrolhos Marine National Park, which is one of the most important marine conservation areas in Brazil. The interior of the region is also home to several important agricultural areas, including the Recôncavo region, which is known for its sugarcane and tobacco production.

The landscape of Bahia is also marked by several unique geological features, including the **Chapada Diamantina National Park**, which is known for its stunning rock formations and waterfalls. The region is also home to several important archaeological sites, including the **São Francisco River Valley**, which is known for its ancient rock art and fossils.
