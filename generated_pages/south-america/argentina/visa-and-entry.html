Visa Policy Overview

Argentina has a moderate visa policy, allowing citizens of many countries to enter without a visa or with a visa on arrival. The country provides visa-free access to citizens of around 90 countries, including most European nations, the United States, Canada, Australia, and Japan. This liberal approach to visa policy makes Argentina an attractive destination for tourists and business travelers alike. The country's visa policy is designed to promote tourism, trade, and cultural exchange, while also ensuring the security and integrity of its borders.

Argentina's visa policy is divided into several categories, including tourist, business, student, and work visas. The requirements and regulations for each category vary, but the overall goal is to facilitate the entry and stay of foreign nationals in Argentina while maintaining national security and public order. Citizens of countries that do not have a visa-free agreement with Argentina may need to apply for a visa at an Argentine embassy or consulate before traveling to the country.

Tourists: Tourists can stay in Argentina for up to 90 days without a visa, depending on their nationality. To enter the country, tourists must have a valid passport, proof of onward travel, and sufficient funds for their stay. Extensions of stay are possible, but must be applied for at the National Immigration Office. Business Travelers: Business travelers may need to provide additional documentation, such as an invitation letter from an Argentine company or a certificate of registration with the Argentine Chamber of Commerce. They may also need to apply for a special type of visa, such as a Business Visa, which requires a longer processing time and additional fees. Students: Students must provide proof of enrollment at an Argentine university or institution, as well as a valid passport and sufficient funds for their stay. They may need to apply for a Student Visa, which requires a longer processing time and additional fees. Workers: Workers must have a job offer from an Argentine employer and apply for a Work Visa, which requires a longer processing time and additional fees. They must also provide proof of qualifications and experience, as well as a medical certificate and police clearance.

The main entry points for Argentina are the international airports in Buenos Aires, such as Ezeiza International Airport and Jorge Newbery Airfield. Land borders are also open, with major crossings at the borders with Chile, Bolivia, Paraguay, Brazil, and Uruguay. Upon arrival, travelers must present their passport and required documents to the immigration officer, who will review and stamp the passport. The officer may also ask questions about the purpose of the visit, the length of stay, and the accommodation arrangements. Travelers must also declare any goods they are bringing with them, such as gifts, souvenirs, or electronic devices.
At the immigration desk, travelers will be given a Tourist Card, which must be presented when departing the country. The card will be stamped with the date of entry and the length of stay, and must be kept safe throughout the stay in Argentina. Travelers who lose their Tourist Card may need to apply for a replacement at the National Immigration Office, which may incur additional fees and delays.
- Not having a valid passport with at least six months' validity
- Not having proof of onward travel or sufficient funds for the duration of stay
- Not completing the visa application form correctly or providing required documentation
- Not declaring goods or gifts at the border
- Not keeping the Tourist Card safe and presenting it when departing the country
- Not respecting the terms and conditions of the visa or stay, such as working without a Work Visa or overstaying the allowed period.
