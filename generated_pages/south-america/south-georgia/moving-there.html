To move to South Georgia, you will need to carefully plan and prepare for the unique challenges and opportunities presented by this remote and rugged British overseas territory in the southern Atlantic Ocean.

Why People Move to South Georgia

South Georgia is a remote and inhospitable island, but it still attracts a small number of expats and visitors who are drawn to its stunning natural beauty, diverse wildlife, and opportunities for scientific research and conservation work. The majority of expats in South Georgia are British citizens, and they are typically employed by the British government or by organizations involved in scientific research, fishing, or tourism. The quality of life in South Georgia is rugged and challenging, with limited access to amenities and services, but it can also be very rewarding for those who are able to adapt to the harsh climate and remote location.

The population of South Georgia is very small, with only a handful of residents living on the island at any given time. The island has no indigenous population, and the only people living there are expats and scientists who are temporarily stationed on the island. Despite the challenges, many expats find the experience of living in South Georgia to be incredibly rewarding, with opportunities to experience the island's unique wildlife and landscapes, and to contribute to important scientific research and conservation efforts.

The expat community in South Georgia is very small and close-knit, with most residents living and working together at the British research station at King Edward Point. The community is typically made up of scientists, researchers, and support staff, as well as a few officials and administrators. The quality of life in South Georgia is generally good, with access to basic amenities and services, but it can be limited by the island's remote location and harsh climate.
