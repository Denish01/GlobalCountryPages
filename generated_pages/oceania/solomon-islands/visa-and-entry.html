Visa Policy Overview

The Solomon Islands has a moderate visa policy, allowing citizens of certain countries to enter visa-free or obtain a visa on arrival. The country is a popular destination for tourists and travelers, with its lush islands, coral reefs, and rich cultural heritage. Currently, citizens of over 30 countries can enter the Solomon Islands without a visa, while others may need to obtain a visa in advance or on arrival.

The Solomon Islands government has implemented measures to facilitate travel and tourism, including the introduction of a visa on arrival system for eligible nationalities. However, it is essential to note that visa requirements can change, and travelers should always check with the official embassy or consulate of Solomon Islands for the most up-to-date information. The country's visa policy aims to balance the need to facilitate travel and tourism with the need to ensure national security and public safety.

By Visitor Type

Tourists: Tourists can enter the Solomon Islands without a visa if they are eligible for a visa-free stay. The typical stay for tourists is 90 days, and extensions can be applied for at the immigration office in Honiara. Tourists must have a valid passport, proof of onward travel, and sufficient funds to support their stay.

Business Travelers: Business travelers may need to obtain a visa in advance, depending on their nationality. They must provide an invitation letter from a local company or organization, as well as proof of their business activities in the Solomon Islands. Business travelers may be required to register with the local authorities and obtain a work permit.

Students: Students must obtain a student visa before entering the Solomon Islands. They must provide proof of enrollment at a recognized educational institution, as well as sufficient funds to support their stay. Student visas are typically valid for the duration of the course of study.

Workers: Workers must obtain a work permit and a visa before entering the Solomon Islands. They must have a job offer from a local employer and meet the relevant qualification and experience requirements. The work permit and visa application process can take several weeks, and workers must register with the local authorities and obtain a tax identification number.

Documents Checklist

  1. Valid passport with at least six months' validity
  2. Completed visa application form (if required)
  3. Recent passport-sized photos
  4. Proof of onward travel (e.g., flight itinerary or travel agency booking)
  5. Proof of sufficient funds (e.g., cash, traveler's checks, or credit cards)
  6. Hotel reservation or confirmation of accommodation
  7. Health insurance certificate (if required)
  8. Police certificate (if required)
  9. Proof of business activities or invitation letter (for business travelers)
  10. Proof of enrollment or student ID (for students)

Entry Points & Procedures

The main entry points to the Solomon Islands are Honiara International Airport and the ports of Honiara and Gizo. Upon arrival, travelers must present their travel documents and complete an arrival card. Immigration officers will inspect the documents and may ask questions about the purpose of the visit, accommodation, and onward travel plans. Travelers must also declare any goods they are bringing with them, including gifts, souvenirs, and food items. The immigration process typically takes around 30 minutes to an hour, depending on the number of travelers and the complexity of the application.

Travelers can also enter the Solomon Islands by sea, either by private yacht or by ferry from neighboring countries. In this case, they must report to the nearest immigration office or port authority and complete the necessary formalities. The Solomon Islands has a relatively straightforward immigration process, but travelers should be prepared to provide detailed information about their visit and comply with local regulations.

Common Mistakes To Avoid

  • Failing to check the visa requirements for their nationality before traveling
  • Not having a valid passport with at least six months' validity
  • Not providing proof of onward travel or sufficient funds
  • Not declaring goods or gifts upon arrival
  • Not registering with the local authorities or obtaining the necessary permits
  • Not respecting local customs and traditions, particularly in rural areas
