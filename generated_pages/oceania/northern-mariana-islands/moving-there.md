# Moving to Northern Mariana Islands — Visas, Residency & Expat Guide

To move to the Northern Mariana Islands, you will need to research and understand the visa and residency options, relocation process, and cultural nuances of this US territory in Oceania, and carefully plan your relocation to ensure a smooth transition to island life.

[SECTION] Why People Move to Northern Mariana Islands [/SECTION]
The Northern Mariana Islands, a chain of 14 islands in the Pacific Ocean, attract a diverse range of expats, from **digital nomads** and **retirees** to **entrepreneurs** and **families**, who are drawn to the islands' stunning natural beauty, rich culture, and unique blend of Asian and American influences. The islands' small population of around 47,329 people and relatively low cost of living make it an attractive destination for those seeking a relaxed, island lifestyle. With a growing economy and a range of outdoor activities, including snorkeling, diving, and hiking, the Northern Mariana Islands offer a great quality of life for expats who are willing to adapt to a more laid-back pace.

The expat community in the Northern Mariana Islands is relatively small, but it is growing, with many expats drawn to the islands' **tourism industry**, **healthcare sector**, and **education system**. The islands' capital, Saipan, is the hub of economic activity and is home to many expats, who are attracted to its modern amenities, including shopping centers, restaurants, and entertainment venues. The Northern Mariana Islands also offer a range of cultural events and festivals, including the annual **Saipan Festival**, which celebrates the islands' rich history and cultural heritage.

Overall, the Northern Mariana Islands offer a unique and rewarding experience for expats, with a great quality of life, a range of outdoor activities, and a rich cultural heritage. However, expats should be aware that the islands are prone to **typhoons** and **power outages**, and that the cost of living can be higher than in some other parts of the world.

3. Obtain a **visa** or **residency permit**.
4. Find **accommodation** in Saipan or other parts of the islands.
5. Open a **bank account** and transfer funds.
6. Register with the local **healthcare system**.

9. Enroll in a **language course** to learn Chamorro or Carolinian.

12. Plan for **emergency situations**, such as typhoons and power outages.

[SECTION] Banking & Finances [/SECTION]
- **Bank of Guam** and **First Hawaiian Bank** are two of the main banks in the Northern Mariana Islands, offering a range of **banking services**.
- **Money transfers** can be made through **Western Union** or **MoneyGram**.
- The **US dollar** is the official currency, and **credit cards** are widely accepted.
- **Taxes** are relatively low, with a **gross receipts tax** of 4% and no **income tax**.
- **Health insurance** is mandatory for all residents, and **medical evacuations** may be necessary in emergency situations.

[SECTION] Challenges & Honest Advice [/SECTION]
- **Culture shock** can be a challenge for expats, particularly in rural areas where **traditional customs** are still practiced.
- **Bureaucracy** can be slow and frustrating, particularly when dealing with **government agencies**.
- **Language barriers** can be a challenge, particularly for those who do not speak **Chamorro** or **Carolinian**.
- **Typhoons** and **power outages** can be a challenge, particularly during the **typhoon season**.
- **Limited job opportunities** can be a challenge, particularly in certain **industries**.
- **Island fever** can be a challenge for some expats, particularly those who are used to a more **fast-paced lifestyle**.
