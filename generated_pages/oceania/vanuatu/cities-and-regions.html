How Vanuatu Is Divided

Vanuatu is an archipelago with a total of six provinces, each comprising several islands. The provinces are Malampa, Penama, Sanma, Shefa, Tafea, and Torba. The country's administrative structure is relatively simple, with the national government based in the capital, Port Vila, and provincial governments responsible for local affairs. The provinces are further divided into municipalities and rural areas, with each having its own council and administrative systems. This structure allows for a degree of autonomy and flexibility, enabling local communities to manage their own affairs and respond to local needs.

The division of Vanuatu into provinces is largely based on geographical and cultural factors, with each province having its own distinct character and identity. For example, the province of Tafea is known for its rugged terrain and traditional villages, while the province of Sanma is home to the country's second-largest town, Luganville. This diversity is reflected in the country's languages, customs, and traditions, making Vanuatu a fascinating and culturally rich destination.

Major Cities

Port Vila (49,000)

Role: capital, economic hub, port city

Known For: Port Vila is the commercial and economic center of Vanuatu, with a mix of modern and traditional architecture, and a vibrant cultural scene. The city is also a popular tourist destination, with a range of accommodation options, restaurants, and recreational activities.

Best For: visitors looking for a mix of urban and cultural experiences, with easy access to the rest of the country.

Luganville (13,000)

Role: economic hub, port city

Known For: Luganville is the second-largest town in Vanuatu and is located on the island of Espiritu Santo. The town has a rich history, with many remnants of World War II, and is also a popular base for exploring the surrounding islands and coral reefs.

Best For: visitors interested in history, diving, and snorkeling, with a range of accommodation options and tourist facilities.

Regional Differences

  • The provinces of Malampa and Tafea are known for their rugged terrain and traditional villages, with a strong focus on cultural heritage and community-based tourism.
  • The province of Sanma is home to the country's second-largest town, Luganville, and has a more urban and commercial character, with a range of accommodation options and tourist facilities.
  • The province of Shefa is the most populous and economically developed, with the capital Port Vila and a range of cultural, recreational, and tourist activities.
  • The province of Torba is the most remote and least developed, with a focus on community-based tourism and sustainable development.

Getting Around

Vanuatu has a relatively well-developed transport system, with domestic flights connecting the main islands and provinces. The country's main airport is located in Port Vila, with regular flights to and from Australia, New Zealand, and other Pacific Island nations. Domestic flights are operated by Air Vanuatu, with regular services to Luganville, Sola, and other provincial centers. The country's road network is limited, but there are regular bus services and taxi operators available in the main towns and cities. Driving conditions can be challenging, especially on the outer islands, but car rental operators are available in Port Vila and Luganville. Alternatively, visitors can use boats and ferries to travel between islands, with a range of operators offering scheduled and charter services.
