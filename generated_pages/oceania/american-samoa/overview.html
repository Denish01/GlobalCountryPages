What Is American Samoa?

American Samoa is an unincorporated territory of the United States, located in the southern Pacific Ocean. It is situated in the Oceania region, southeast of Samoa and northwest of Tonga. The territory consists of five main islands: Tutuila, Aunu'u, Ofu, Olosega, and Ta'u. American Samoa is known for its stunning natural beauty, including coral reefs, white-sand beaches, and lush rainforests. The territory has a unique cultural identity, shaped by its Samoan heritage and American influences.

American Samoa has a strategic geographic position, with the Port of Pago Pago serving as a key hub for international shipping and trade. The territory's economy is primarily driven by the tuna canning industry, with the StarKist factory being one of the largest employers. Tourism also plays a significant role in the economy, with visitors drawn to the territory's natural attractions and cultural experiences. Despite its small size, American Samoa has a distinct identity and a strong sense of community, with a blend of traditional and modern ways of life.

The territory's sovereignty status is that of an unincorporated territory, meaning that it is not part of any state and is instead governed directly by the federal government. American Samoa has a local government, with a governor and a legislature, but the US Congress has the authority to review and approve laws passed by the territory. This unique arrangement reflects the territory's complex history and its ongoing relationship with the United States.

Key History

  1. 1900 - The United States annexes American Samoa, following a treaty with Samoa.
  2. 1905 - The US Navy establishes a government in American Samoa, with a naval governor appointed to oversee the territory.
  3. 1940s - American Samoa plays a significant role in World War II, with the territory serving as a military base and supply depot.
  4. 1951 - The US Department of the Interior assumes responsibility for American Samoa, replacing the US Navy.
  5. 1960 - American Samoa adopts a local constitution, providing for a governor and a legislature.
  6. 1980 - The US Congress passes the American Samoa Delegate Act, allowing the territory to send a non-voting delegate to the US House of Representatives.
  7. 2009 - American Samoa is affected by the Tsunami, which causes significant damage and loss of life.

What Makes American Samoa Unique

  • American Samoa is one of the most remote territories in the world, with a unique cultural identity shaped by its Samoan heritage and American influences.
  • The territory is home to the National Park of American Samoa, which covers over 13,000 acres of land and sea.
  • American Samoa has a distinct tuna industry, with the territory being one of the largest tuna producers in the world.
  • The territory has a strong sense of community, with a blend of traditional and modern ways of life.
  • American Samoa is a popular destination for ecotourism, with visitors drawn to the territory's stunning natural beauty and outdoor activities.

American Samoa is a unique and fascinating territory, with a blend of Samoan culture and American influences, stunning natural beauty, and a strong sense of community, making it an attractive destination for tourists and a distinct part of the United States.
