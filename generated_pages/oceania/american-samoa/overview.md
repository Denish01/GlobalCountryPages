[SECTION] What Is American Samoa? [/SECTION]
American Samoa is an unincorporated territory of the United States, located in the southern Pacific Ocean. It is situated in the **Oceania** region, southeast of Samoa and northwest of Tonga. The territory consists of five main islands: Tutuila, Aunu'u, Ofu, Olosega, and Ta'u. American Samoa is known for its stunning natural beauty, including coral reefs, white-sand beaches, and lush rainforests. The territory has a unique cultural identity, shaped by its **Samoan** heritage and American influences.

American Samoa has a strategic geographic position, with the Port of Pago Pago serving as a key hub for international shipping and trade. The territory's economy is primarily driven by the tuna canning industry, with the **StarKist** factory being one of the largest employers. Tourism also plays a significant role in the economy, with visitors drawn to the territory's natural attractions and cultural experiences. Despite its small size, American Samoa has a distinct identity and a strong sense of community, with a blend of traditional and modern ways of life.

The territory's sovereignty status is that of an unincorporated territory, meaning that it is not part of any state and is instead governed directly by the federal government. American Samoa has a local government, with a governor and a legislature, but the US Congress has the authority to review and approve laws passed by the territory. This unique arrangement reflects the territory's complex history and its ongoing relationship with the United States.
