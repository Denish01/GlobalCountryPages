Food Culture Overview

American Samoa's cuisine is a reflection of its rich cultural heritage, with a unique blend of traditional Samoan, Polynesian, Asian, and American influences. The local diet is heavily reliant on fresh seafood, coconuts, and root vegetables like taro and breadfruit. Fa'a Samoa, or the Samoan way, emphasizes the importance of traditional cooking methods, such as steaming and baking in umu (underground ovens). The cuisine is also shaped by the island's history of trade and migration, with popular dishes like Laulau (steamed pork and fish in taro leaves) and Masi (Samoan-style BBQ) showcasing the fusion of local and international flavors.

The food culture in American Samoa is also closely tied to the island's social and ceremonial practices. Traditional Samoan To'ona'i (Sunday lunch) gatherings, for example, feature an array of dishes, including Palusami (steamed taro and coconut cream) and Oka (raw fish marinated in coconut cream and lime juice). These gatherings are an essential part of Samoan social life, fostering community and family bonds.

Regional variations in American Samoa's cuisine are relatively limited, given the small size of the territory. However, the eastern and western districts have distinct differences in their cooking styles and ingredient preferences. The eastern district, for instance, is known for its emphasis on fresh seafood, while the western district favors more traditional, land-based ingredients like taro and breadfruit.

Dining Customs

  • Eating with hands is common in traditional Samoan settings, but utensils are also widely used
  • Respect for elders is essential in Samoan culture, with older individuals often served first
  • Tipping is not mandatory, but 10-15% of the total bill is customary
  • Dress code is casual, but modest clothing is recommended when dining in traditional Samoan settings
  • Reservation culture is not prevalent, but it's a good idea to call ahead to confirm availability, especially during peak hours
  • Food sharing is a common practice in Samoan culture, with dishes often served family-style

Where to Eat

  • Seafood restaurants: serving fresh seafood and stunning ocean views
  • Food courts: offering a variety of international and local cuisine options
  • Markets: featuring fresh produce, meats, and traditional Samoan dishes
  • Beachside restaurants: providing a relaxed and scenic dining experience

Food Markets & Food Experiences

  • Pago Pago Market (Pago Pago): a popular market featuring a range of local food stalls and vendors
  • Tafuna Market (Tafuna): a smaller market offering fresh produce, meats, and traditional Samoan snacks
  • Samoan Cultural Village (Pago Pago): a cultural center offering traditional Samoan cooking classes and demonstrations
