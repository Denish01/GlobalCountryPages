Food Culture Overview

Western Australia's cuisine is defined by its unique blend of coastal and outback influences, with a focus on fresh seafood, high-quality meats, and native bush foods. The region's food culture has been shaped by its history of immigration, with influences from European, Asian, and Indigenous Australian cuisines. From the Aboriginal community's traditional use of bush foods to the Italian and Greek influences on the state's seafood dishes, Western Australia's culinary scene is a vibrant reflection of its cultural diversity. The state's capital, Perth, is a hub for foodies, with a range of restaurants, cafes, and markets showcasing the region's best produce.

The state's south-west region is known for its truffles, with the town of Manjimup hosting an annual truffle festival. The north-west region, including the Kimberley and Pilbara, is famous for its seafood, with popular dishes including fish and chips and seafood paella. The state's outback regions, including the Goldfields and Mid West, offer a range of traditional Australian dishes, including meat pies and scones.

Western Australia's food culture is also characterized by its emphasis on local and seasonal produce, with many restaurants and cafes showcasing the state's best ingredients. The state's farmers' markets, including the Perth City Farm and the Fremantle Markets, offer a range of fresh produce, including fruits, vegetables, and meats. With its unique blend of coastal and outback influences, Western Australia's cuisine is a must-try experience for any food lover.

Dining Customs

  • Tipping: it is customary to tip 5-10% of the total bill at restaurants and cafes
  • Reservations: it is recommended to make reservations at popular restaurants, especially on weekends and holidays
  • Dress Code: the dress code is generally casual, but some restaurants may have a smart casual or formal dress code
  • Eating Etiquette: it is customary to wait for the host to indicate where to sit, and to keep your hands visible on the table
  • Food Sharing: it is common to share food with others, especially at traditional Australian barbecues and picnics
  • Table Manners: it is customary to use a napkin and to not speak with your mouth full
