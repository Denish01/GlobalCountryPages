Why People Move to Papua New Guinea

Papua New Guinea (PNG) is a country with immense cultural and natural diversity, attracting expats and adventurers alike. The most common reasons for moving to PNG include work opportunities in the mining and oil industries, as well as volunteer work with non-governmental organizations (NGOs) and missionary activities. The expat demographic in PNG is relatively small, with most expats residing in the capital city of Port Moresby or other major urban centers like Lae and Mount Hagen. In terms of quality of life, PNG offers a unique blend of traditional and modern amenities, with a relatively low cost of living compared to other countries in the Pacific region.

Despite the challenges, many expats find PNG to be a rewarding and enriching experience, with opportunities to engage with local communities and experience the country's incredible natural beauty. The country's tropical climate and stunning landscapes make it an attractive destination for outdoor enthusiasts, while the vibrant cultural scene and friendly locals make it an excellent choice for those looking to immerse themselves in a new culture.

However, it is essential to note that PNG is a developing country with significant infrastructure challenges and security concerns, which can impact the overall quality of life for expats. As such, it is crucial for prospective expats to carefully weigh the pros and cons of moving to PNG and to thoroughly research the country's unique challenges and opportunities.

Relocation Checklist

  1. Research and understand PNG's culture, customs, and way of life
  2. Determine the type of visa required and gather necessary documents
  3. Secure accommodation in Port Moresby or other major urban centers
  4. Arrange for health insurance that covers medical evacuation
  5. Plan for security measures, such as hiring a guard or using a secure vehicle
  6. Open a local bank account to manage finances and receive payments
  7. Register with the local authorities and obtain any necessary permits
  8. Find a reliable transportation option, such as a car or driver
  9. Connect with expat communities and join online forums or social groups
  10. Plan for emergency situations, such as natural disasters or medical emergencies
  11. Research and understand the local tax laws and regulations
  12. Consider hiring a relocation consultant to assist with the move

Expat Communities

  • The majority of expats reside in Port Moresby, with smaller communities in Lae and Mount Hagen
  • Online groups, such as the Expats in PNG Facebook group, provide a platform for expats to connect and share information
  • Social networks, such as Internations, offer a range of resources and events for expats in PNG
  • Meetups, such as the Port Moresby Expats group, organize regular social events and activities
  • The Australian High Commission and other diplomatic missions often host events and provide support for expats

Banking & Finances

  • Opening a local bank account is relatively straightforward, with several major banks operating in PNG
  • Money transfers can be made through Western Union or other international money transfer services
  • The Papua New Guinea Kina (PGK) is the local currency, although Australian dollars and US dollars are widely accepted
  • Credit cards, such as Visa and Mastercard, are accepted at major hotels and restaurants
  • It is essential to understand the local tax laws and regulations, as well as any tax implications for expats

Challenges & Honest Advice

  • Culture shock can be significant, particularly for those from Western countries
  • Bureaucracy and red tape can be frustrating, with slow processing times for official documents
  • Language barriers can be a challenge, particularly outside of major urban centers
  • Security concerns, such as crime and violence, are a significant issue in PNG
  • Infrastructure challenges, such as power outages and poor road conditions, can impact daily life
  • It is essential to be flexible and patient when dealing with the challenges of living in PNG, and to seek support from expat communities and local authorities when needed
